A Funeral Elegy

  1. The love I bore to your brother, and will do to his memory, hath crav'd from me this last duty of a friend; I am herein but a second to the privilege of Truth, who can warrant more in his behalf than I undertook to deliver. Exercise in this kind I will little affect, and am less addicted to, but there must be miracle in that labor which, to witness my remembrance to this departed gentleman, I would not willingly undergo. Yet whatsoever is here done, is done to him, and to him only. For whom and whose sake I will not forget to remember any friendly respects to you, or to any of those that have lov'd him for himself, and himself for his deserts.
  2. Since Time, and his predestinated end,
  3. Abridg’d the circuit of his hopeful days,
  4. Whiles both his Youth and Virtue did intend
  5. The good endeavors of deserving praise,
  6. What memorable monument can last
  7. Whereon to build his never-blemish’d name
  8. But his own worth, wherein his life was grac’d—
  9. Sith as [that] ever he maintain’d the same?
  10. Oblivion in the darkest day to come,
  11. When sin shall tread on merit in the dust,
  12. Cannot rase out the lamentable tomb
  13. Of his short-liv’d deserts; but still they must,
  14. Even in the hearts and memories of men,
  15. Claim fit Respect, that they, in every limb
  16. Rememb’ring what he was, with comfort then
  17. May pattern out one truly good, by him.
  18. For he was truly good, if honest care
  19. Of harmless conversation may commend
  20. A life free from such stains as follies are,
  21. Ill recompensed only in his end.
  22. Nor can the tongue of him who lov’d him least
  23. (If there can be minority of love
  24. To one superlative above the rest
  25. Of many men in steady faith) reprove
  26. His constant temper, in the equal weight
  27. Of thankfulness and kindness: Truth doth leave
  28. Sufficient proof, he was in every right
  29. As kind to give, as thankful to receive.
  30. The curious eye of a quick-brain’d survey
  31. Could scantly find a mote amidst the sun
  32. Of his too-short’ned days, or make a prey
  33. Of any faulty errors he had done—
  34. Not that he was above the spleenful sense
  35. And spite of malice, but for that he had
  36. Warrant enough in his own innocence
  37. Against the sting of some in nature bad.
  38. Yet who is he so absolutely blest
  39. That lives encompass’d in a mortal frame,
  40. Sometime in reputation not oppress’d
  41. By some in nothing famous but defame?
  42. Such in the By-path and the Ridgeway lurk
  43. That leads to ruin, in a smooth pretense
  44. Of what they do to be a special work
  45. Of singleness, not tending to offense;
  46. Whose very virtues are, not to detract
  47. Whiles hope remains of gain (base fee of slaves),
  48. Despising chiefly men in fortunes wrack’d—
  49. But death to such gives unrememb’red graves.
  50. Now therein liv’d he happy, if to be
  51. Free from detraction happiness it be.
  52. His younger years gave comfortable hope
  53. To hope for comfort in his riper youth,
  54. Which, harvest-like, did yield again the crop
  55. Of Education, better’d in his truth.
  56. Those noble twins of heaven-infused races,
  57. Learning and Wit, refined in their kind
  58. Did jointly both, in their peculiar graces,
  59. Enrich the curious temple of his mind;
  60. Indeed a temple, in whose precious white
  61. Sat Reason by Religion oversway’d,
  62. Teaching his other senses, with delight,
  63. How Piety and Zeal should be obey’d—
  64. Not fruitlessly in prodigal expense
  65. Wasting his best of time, but so content
  66. With Reason’s golden Mean to make defense
  67. Against the assault of youth’s encouragement;
  68. As not the tide of this surrounding age
  69. (When now his father’s death had freed his will)
  70. Could make him subject to the drunken rage
  71. Of such whose only glory is their ill.
  72. He from the happy knowledge of the wise
  73. Draws virtue to reprove secured fools
  74. And shuns the glad sleights of ensnaring vice
  75. To spend his spring of days in sacred schools.
  76. Here gave he diet to the sick desires
  77. That day by day assault the weaker man,
  78. And with fit moderation still retires
  79. From what doth batter virtue now and then.
  80. But that I not intend in full discourse
  81. To progress out his life, I could display
  82. A good man in each part exact and force
  83. The common voice to warrant what I say.
  84. For if his fate and heaven had decreed
  85. That full of days he might have liv’d to see
  86. The grave in peace, the times that should succeed
  87. Had been best-speaking witnesses with me;
  88. Whose conversation so untouch’d did move
  89. Respect most in itself, as who would scan
  90. His honesty and worth, by them might prove
  91. He was a kind, true, perfect gentleman—
  92. Not in the outside of disgraceful folly,
  93. Courting opinion with unfit disguise,
  94. Affecting fashions, nor addicted wholly
  95. To unbeseeming blushless vanities,
  96. But suiting so his habit and desire
  97. As that his Virtue was his best Attire.
  98. Not in the waste of many idle words
  99. Car’d he to be heard talk, nor in the float
  100. Of fond conceit, such as this age affords,
  101. By vain discourse upon himself to dote;
  102. For his becoming silence gave such grace
  103. To his judicious parts, as what he spake
  104. Seem’d rather answers which the wise embrace
  105. Than busy questions such as talkers make.
  106. And though his qualities might well deserve
  107. Just commendation, yet his furnish’d mind
  108. Such harmony of goodness did preserve
  109. As nature never built in better kind;
  110. Knowing the best, and therefore not presuming
  111. In knowing, but for that it was the best,
  112. Ever within himself free choice resuming
  113. Of true perfection, in a perfect breast;
  114. So that his mind and body made an inn,
  115. The one to lodge the other, both like fram’d
  116. For fair conditions, guests that soonest win
  117. Applause; in generality, well fam’d,
  118. If trim behavior, gestures mild, discreet
  119. Endeavors, modest speech, beseeming mirth,
  120. True friendship, active grace, persuasion sweet,
  121. Delightful love innated from his birth,
  122. Acquaintance unfamiliar, carriage just,
  123. Offenseless resolution, wish’d sobriety,
  124. Clean-temper’d moderation, steady trust,
  125. Unburthen’d conscience, unfeign’d piety;
  126. If these, or all of these, knit fast in one
  127. Can merit praise, then justly may we say,
  128. Not any from this frailer stage is gone
  129. Whose name is like to live a longer day—
  130. Though not in eminent courts or places great
  131. For popular concourse, yet in that soil
  132. Where he enjoy’d his birth, life, death, and seat
  133. Which now sits mourning his untimely spoil. A
  134. nd as much glory is it to be good
  135. For private persons, in their private home,
  136. As those descended from illustrious blood
  137. In public view of greatness, whence they come.
  138. Though I, rewarded with some sadder taste
  139. Of knowing shame, by feeling it have prov’d
  140. My country’s thankless misconstruction cast
  141. Upon my name and credit, both unlov’d
  142. By some whose fortunes, sunk into the wane
  143. Of plenty and desert, have strove to win
  144. Justice by wrong, and sifted to embane
  145. My reputation with a witless sin;
  146. Yet time, the father of unblushing truth,
  147. May one day lay ope malice which hath cross’d it,
  148. And right the hopes of my endangered youth,
  149. Purchasing credit in the place I lost it.
  150. Even in which place the subject of the verse
  151. (Unhappy matter of a mourning style
  152. Which now that subject’s merits doth rehearse)
  153. Had education and new being; while
  154. By fair demeanor he had won repute
  155. Amongst the all of all that lived there,
  156. For that his actions did so wholly suit
  157. With worthiness, still memorable here.
  158. The many hours till the day of doom
  159. Will not consume his life and hapless end,
  160. For should he lie obscur’d without a tomb,
  161. Time would to time his honesty commend;
  162. Whiles parents to their children will make known,
  163. And they to their posterity impart,
  164. How such a man was sadly overthrown
  165. By a hand guided by a cruel heart,
  166. Whereof as many as shall hear that sadness
  167. Will blame the one’s hard fate, the other’s madness;
  168. Whiles such as do recount that tale of woe,
  169. Told by remembrance of the wisest heads,
  170. Will in the end conclude the matter so,
  171. As they will all go weeping to their beds.
  172. For when the world lies winter’d in the storms
  173. Of fearful consummation, and lays down
  174. Th’ unsteady change of his fantastic forms,
  175. Expecting ever to be overthrown;
  176. When the proud height of much affected sin
  177. Shall ripen to a head, and in that pride
  178. End in the miseries it did begin
  179. And fall amidst the glory of his tide;
  180. Then in a book where every work is writ
  181. Shall this man’s actions be reveal’d, to show
  182. The gainful fruit of well-employed wit,
  183. Which paid to heaven the debt that it did owe.
  184. Here shall be reckon’d up the constant faith,
  185. Never untrue, where once he love profess’d;
  186. Which is a miracle in men, one saith,
  187. Long sought though rarely found, and he is best
  188. Who can make friendship, in those times of change,
  189. Admired more for being firm than strange.
  190. When those weak houses of our brittle flesh
  191. Shall ruin’d be by death, our grace and strength,
  192. Youth, memory and shape that made us fresh
  193. Cast down, and utterly decay’d at length;
  194. When all shall turn to dust from whence we came
  195. And we low-level’d in a narrow grave,
  196. What can we leave behind us but a name,
  197. Which, by a life well led, may honor have?
  198. Such honor, O thou youth untimely lost,
  199. Thou didst deserve and hast; for though thy soul
  200. Hath took her flight to a diviner coast,
  201. Yet here on earth thy fame lives ever whole,
  202. In every heart seal’d up, in every tongue
  203. Fit matter to discourse, no day prevented
  204. That pities not thy sad and sudden wrong,
  205. Of all alike beloved and lamented.
  206. And I here to thy memorable worth,
  207. In this last act of friendship, sacrifice
  208. My love to thee, which I could not set forth
  209. In any other habit of disguise.
  210. Although I could not learn, whiles yet thou wert,
  211. To speak the language of a servile breath,
  212. My truth stole from my tongue into my heart,
  213. Which shall not thence be sund’red, but in death.
  214. And I confess my love was too remiss
  215. That had not made thee know how much I priz’d thee,
  216. But that mine error was, as yet it is,
  217. To think love best in silence: for I siz’d thee
  218. By what I would have been, not only ready
  219. In telling I was thine, but being so,
  220. By some effect to show it. He is steady
  221. Who seems less than he is in open show.
  222. Since then I still reserv’d to try the worst
  223. Which hardest fate and time thus can lay on me.
  224. T’ enlarge my thoughts was hindered at first,
  225. While thou hadst life; I took this task upon me,
  226. To register with mine unhappy pen
  227. Such duties as it owes to thy desert,
  228. And set thee as a president to men,
  229. And limn thee to the world but as thou wert—
  230. Not hir’d, as heaven can witness in my soul,
  231. By vain conceit, to please such ones as know it,
  232. Nor servile to be lik’d, free from control,
  233. Which, pain to many men, I do not owe it.
  234. But here I trust I have discharged now
  235. (Fair lovely branch too soon cut off) to thee,
  236. My constant and irrefragable vow,
  237. As, had it chanc’d, thou mightst have done to me—
  238. But that no merit strong enough of mine
  239. Had yielded store to thy well-abled quill
  240. Whereby t’enroll my name, as this of thine,
  241. How s’ere enriched by thy plenteous skill.
  242. Here, then, I offer up to memory
  243. The value of my talent, precious man,
  244. Whereby if thou live to posterity,
  245. Though’t be not as I would, ’tis as I can:
  246. In minds from whence endeavor doth proceed,
  247. A ready will is taken for the deed.
  248. Yet ere I take my longest last farewell
  249. From thee, fair mark of sorrow, let me frame
  250. Some ampler work of thank, wherein to tell
  251. What more thou didst deserve than in thy name,
  252. And free thee from the scandal of such senses
  253. As in the rancor of unhappy spleen
  254. Measure thy course of life, with false pretenses
  255. Comparing by thy death what thou hast been.
  256. So in his mischiefs is the world accurs’d:
  257. It picks out matter to inform the worst.
  258. The willful blindness that hoodwinks the eyes
  259. Of men enwrapped in an earthy veil
  260. Makes them most ignorantly exercise
  261. And yield to humor when it doth assail,
  262. Whereby the candle and the body’s light
  263. Darkens the inward eyesight of the mind,
  264. Presuming still it sees, even in the night
  265. Of that same ignorance which makes them blind.
  266. Hence conster they with corrupt commentaries,
  267. Proceeding from a nature as corrupt,
  268. The text of malice, which so often varies
  269. As ’tis by seeming reason underpropp’d.
  270. O, whither tends the lamentable spite
  271. Of this world’s teenful apprehension,
  272. Which understands all things amiss, whose light
  273. Shines not amidst the dark of their dissension?
  274. True ’tis, this man, whiles yet he was a man,
  275. Sooth’d not the current of besotted fashion,
  276. Nor could digest, as some loose mimics can,
  277. An empty sound of overweening passion,
  278. So much to be made servant to the base
  279. And sensual aptness of disunion’d vices,
  280. To purchase commendation by disgrace,
  281. Whereto the world and heat of sin entices.
  282. But in a safer contemplation,
  283. Secure in what he knew, he ever chose
  284. The ready way to commendation,
  285. By shunning all invitements strange, of those
  286. Whose illness is, the necessary praise
  287. Must wait upon their actions; only rare
  288. In being rare in shame (which strives to raise
  289. Their name by doing what they do not care),
  290. As if the free commission of their ill
  291. Were even as boundless as their prompt desires;
  292. Only like lords, like subjects to their will,
  293. Which their fond dotage ever more admires.
  294. He was not so: but in a serious awe,
  295. Ruling the little ordered commonwealth
  296. Of his own self, with honor to the law
  297. That gave peace to his bread, bread to his health;
  298. Which ever he maintain’d in sweet content
  299. And pleasurable rest, wherein he joy’d
  300. A monarchy of comfort’s government,
  301. Never until his last to be destroy’d.
  302. For in the Vineyard of heaven-favored learning
  303. Where he was double-honor’d in degree,
  304. His observation and discreet discerning
  305. Had taught him in both fortunes to be free;
  306. Whence now retir’d home, to a home indeed
  307. The home of his condition and estate,
  308. He well provided ’gainst the hand of need,
  309. Whence young men sometime grow unfortunate;
  310. His disposition, by the bonds of unity,
  311. So fast’ned to his reason that it strove
  312. With understanding’s grave immunity
  313. To purchase from all hearts a steady love;
  314. Wherein not any one thing comprehends
  315. Proportionable note of what he was,
  316. Than that he was so constant to his friends
  317. As he would no occasion overpass
  318. Which might make known his unaffected care,
  319. In all respects of trial, to unlock
  320. His bosom and his store, which did declare
  321. That Christ was his, and he was Friendship’s Rock:
  322. A Rock of Friendship figured in his name,
  323. Fore-shewing what he was, and what should be,
  324. Most true presage; and he discharg’d the same
  325. In every act of perfect amity—
  326. Though in the complemental phrase of words
  327. He never was addicted to the vain
  328. Of boast, such as the common breath affords;
  329. He was in use most fast, in tongue most plain,
  330. Nor amongst all those virtues that forever
  331. Adorn’d his reputation will be found
  332. One greater than his Faith, which did persever,
  333. Where once it was protested, alway sound.
  334. Hence sprung the deadly fuel that reviv’d
  335. The rage which wrought his end, for had he been
  336. Slacker in love, he had been longer liv’d
  337. And not oppress’d by wrath’s unhappy sin—
  338. By wrath’s unhappy sin, which unadvis’d
  339. Gave death for free good will, and wounds for love.
  340. Pity it was that blood had not been priz’d
  341. At higher rate, and reason set above
  342. Most unjust choler, which untimely
  343. Drew Destruction on itself; and most unjust,
  344. Robb’d virtue of a follower so true
  345. As time can boast of, both for love and trust:
  346. So henceforth all (great glory to his blood)
  347. Shall be but seconds to him, being good.
  348. The wicked end their honor with their sin
  349. In death, which only then the good begin.
  350. Lo, here a lesson by experience taught
  351. For men whose pure simplicity hath drawn
  352. Their trust to be betray’d by being caught
  353. Within the snares of making truth a pawn;
  354. Whiles it, not doubting whereinto it enters,
  355. Without true proof and knowledge of a friend,
  356. Sincere in singleness of heart, adventers
  357. To give fit cause, ere love begin to end:
  358. His unfeign’d friendship where it least was sought,
  359. Him to a fatal timeless ruin brought;
  360. Whereby the life that purity adorn’d
  361. With real merit, by this sudden end
  362. Is in the mouth of some in manner scorn’d,
  363. Made questionable, for they do intend,
  364. According to the tenor of the saw
  365. Mistook, if not observ’d (writ long ago
  366. When men were only led by Reason’s law),
  367. That "Such as is the end, the life proves so."
  368. Thus he, who to the universal lapse
  369. Gave sweet redemption, off’ring up his blood
  370. To conquer death by death, and loose the traps
  371. Of hell, even in the triumph that it stood:
  372. He thus, for that his guiltless life was spilt
  373. By death, which was made subject to the curse,
  374. Might in like manner be reprov’d of guilt
  375. In his pure life, for that his end was worse.
  376. But O far be it, our unholy lips
  377. Should so profane the deity above
  378. As thereby to ordain revenging whips
  379. Against the day of Judgment and of Love.
  380. The hand that lends us honor in our days
  381. May shorten when it please, and justly take
  382. Our honor from us many sundry ways,
  383. As best becomes that wisdom did us make.
  384. The second brother, who was next begot
  385. Of all that ever were begotten yet,
  386. Was by a hand in vengeance rude and hot
  387. Sent innocent to be in heaven set—
  388. Whose fame the angels in melodious choirs
  389. Still witness to the world. Then why should he,
  390. Well-profited in excellent desires,
  391. Be more rebuk’d, who had like destiny?
  392. Those saints before the everlasting throne
  393. Who sit with crowns of glory on their heads,
  394. Wash’d white in blood, from earth hence have not gone
  395. All to their joys in quiet on their beds,
  396. But tasted of the sour-bitter scourge
  397. Of torture and affliction ere they gained
  398. Those blessings which their sufferance did urge,
  399. Whereby the grace fore-promis’d they attained.
  400. Let then the false suggestions of the froward,
  401. Building large castles in the empty air,
  402. By suppositions fond and thoughts untoward
  403. (Issues of discontent and sick despair)
  404. Rebound gross arguments upon their heart
  405. That may disprove their malice, and confound
  406. Uncivil loose opinions which insert
  407. Their souls into the roll that doth unsound
  408. Betraying policies, and show their brains,
  409. Unto their shame, ridiculous; whose scope
  410. Is envy, whose endeavors fruitless pains,
  411. In nothing surely prosperous, but hope—
  412. And that same hope, so lame, so unprevailing,
  413. It buries self-conceit in weak opinion;
  414. Which being cross’d, gives matter of bewailing
  415. Their vain designs, on whom want hath dominion.
  416. Such, and of such condition, may devise
  417. Which way to wound with defamation’s spirit
  418. (Close-lurking whisper’s hidden forgeries)
  419. His taintless goodness, his desertful merit.
  420. But whiles the minds of men can judge sincerely,
  421. Upon assured knowledge, his repute
  422. And estimation shall be rumor’d clearly
  423. In equal worth—time shall to time renew ’t.
  424. The Grave-that in his ever-empty womb
  425. Forever closes up the unrespected
  426. Who, when they die, die all—shall not entomb
  427. His pleading best perfections as neglected.
  428. They to his notice in succeeding years
  429. Shall speak for him when he shall lie below;
  430. When nothing but his memory appears
  431. Of what he was, then shall his virtues grow.
  432. His being but a private man in rank
  433. (And yet not rank’d beneath a gentleman)
  434. Shall not abridge the commendable thank
  435. Which wise posterity shall give him then;
  436. For Nature, and his therein happy Fate.
  437. Ordain’d that by his quality of mind
  438. T’ ennoble that best part, although his state
  439. Were to a lower blessedness confin’d.
  440. Blood, pomp, state, honor, glory and command,
  441. Without fit ornaments of disposition,
  442. Are in themselves but heathenish and profane,
  443. And much more peaceful is a mean condition
  444. Which, underneath the roof of safe content,
  445. Feeds on the bread of rest, and takes delight
  446. To look upon the labors it hath spent
  447. For its own sustenance, both day and night;
  448. Whiles others, plotting which way to be great,
  449. How to augment their portion and ambition,
  450. Do toil their giddy brains, and ever sweat
  451. For popular applause and power’s commission.
  452. But one in honors, like a sealed dove
  453. Whose inward eyes are dimm’d with dignity,
  454. Does think most safety doth remain above,
  455. And seeks to be secure by mounting high:
  456. Whence, when he falls, who did erewhile aspire,
  457. Falls deeper down, for that he climbed higher.
  458. Now men who in a lower region live
  459. Exempt from danger of authority
  460. Have fittest times in Reason’s rules to thrive,
  461. Not vex’d with envy of priority,
  462. And those are much more noble in the mind
  463. Than many that have nobleness by kind.
  464. Birth, blood, and ancestors, are none of ours,
  465. Nor can we make a proper challenge to them,
  466. But virtues and perfections in our powers
  467. Proceed most truly from us, if we do them.
  468. Respective titles or a gracious style,
  469. With all what men in eminence possess,
  470. Are, without ornaments to praise them, vile:
  471. The beauty of the mind is nobleness.
  472. And such as have that beauty, well deserve
  473. Eternal characters, that after death
  474. Remembrance of their worth we may preserve,
  475. So that their glory die not with their breath.
  476. Else what avails it in a goodly strife
  477. Upon this face of earth here to contend,
  478. The good t’exceed the wicked in their life,
  479. Should both be like obscured in their end?
  480. Until which end, there is none rightly can
  481. Be termed happy, since the happiness
  482. Depends upon the goodness of the man,
  483. Which afterwards his praises will express.
  484. Look hither then, you that enjoy the youth
  485. Of your best days, and see how unexpected
  486. Death can betray your jollity to ruth
  487. When death you think is least to be respected!
  488. The person of this model here set out
  489. Had all that youth and happy days could give him,
  490. Yet could not all-encompass him about
  491. Against th’assault of death, who to relieve him
  492. Struck home but to the frail and mortal parts
  493. Of his humanity, but could not touch
  494. His flourishing and fair long-liv’d deserts,
  495. Above fate’s reach, his singleness was such—
  496. So that he dies but once, but doubly lives,
  497. Once in his proper self, then in his name;
  498. Predestinated Time, who all deprives,
  499. Could never yet deprive him of the same.
  500. And had the Genius which attended on him
  501. Been possibilited to keep him safe
  502. Against the rigor that hath overgone him,
  503. He had been to the public use a staff,
  504. Leading by his example in the path
  505. Which guides to doing well, wherein so few
  506. The proneness of this age to error hath
  507. Informed rightly in the courses true.
  508. As then the loss of one, whose inclination
  509. Strove to win love in general, is sad,
  510. So specially his friends, in soft compassion
  511. Do feel the greatest loss they could have had.
  512. Amongst them all, she who those nine of years
  513. Liv’d fellow to his counsels and his bed
  514. Hath the most share in loss: for I in hers
  515. Feel what distemperature this chance hath bred.
  516. The chaste embracements of conjugal love,
  517. Who in a mutual harmony consent,
  518. Are so impatient of a strange remove
  519. As meager death itself seems to lament,
  520. And weep upon those cheeks which nature fram’d
  521. To be delightful orbs in whom the force
  522. Of lively sweetness plays, so that asham’d
  523. Death often pities his unkind divorce.
  524. Such was the separation here constrain’d
  525. (Well-worthy to be termed a rudeness rather),
  526. For in his life his love was so unfeign’d
  527. As he was both an husband and a father—
  528. The one in firm affection and the other
  529. In careful providence, which ever strove
  530. With joint assistance to grace one another
  531. With every helpful furtherance of love.
  532. But since the sum of all that can be said
  533. Can be but said that "He was good"
  534. (which wholly Includes all excellence can be display’d
  535. In praise of virtue and reproach of folly).
  536. His due deserts, this sentence on him gives,
  537. "He died in life, yet in his death he lives."
  538. Now runs the method of this doleful song
  539. In accents brief to thee, O thou deceas’d!
  540. To whom those pains do only all belong
  541. As witnesses I did not love thee least.
  542. For could my worthless brain find out but how
  543. To raise thee from the sepulcher of dust,
  544. Undoubtedly thou shouldst have partage now
  545. Of life with me, and heaven be counted just
  546. If to a supplicating soul it would
  547. Give life anew, by giving life again
  548. Where life is miss’d; whereby discomfort should
  549. Right his old griefs, and former joys retain
  550. Which now with thee are leap’d into thy tomb
  551. And buried in that hollow vault of woe,
  552. Expecting yet a more severer doom
  553. Than time’s strict flinty hand will let ’em know.
  554. And now if I have level’d mine account
  555. And reckon’d up in a true measured score
  556. Those perfect graces which were ever wont
  557. To wait on thee alive, I ask no more
  558. (But shall hereafter in a poor content
  559. Immure those imputations I sustain,
  560. Learning my days of youth so to prevent
  561. As not to be cast down by them again)—
  562. Only those hopes which fate denies to grant
  563. In full possession to a captive heart
  564. Who, if it were in plenty, still would want
  565. Before it may enjoy his better part;
  566. From which detain’d, and banish’d in th’ exile
  567. Of dim misfortune, has none other prop
  568. Whereon to lean and rest itself the while
  569. But the weak comfort of the hapless, Hope.
  570. And Hope must in despite of fearful change
  571. Play in the strongest closet of my breast,
  572. Although perhaps I ignorantly range
  573. And court opinion in my deep’st unrest.
  574. But whether doth the stream of my mischance
  575. Drive me beyond myself, fast friend, soon lost,
  576. Long may thy worthiness thy name advance
  577. Amongst the virtuous and deserving most,
  578. Who herein hast forever happy prov’d:
  579. In life thou liv’dst, in death thou died’st belov’d.
