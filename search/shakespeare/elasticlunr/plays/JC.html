SCENE Rome: the neighbourhood of Sardis: the neighbourhood of Philippi.

ACT I

SCENE I. Rome. A street.

Enter FLAVIUS, MARULLUS, and certain Commoners
  1. FLAVIUS
  2. Hence! home, you idle creatures get you home:
  3. Is this a holiday? what! know you not,
  4. Being mechanical, you ought not walk
  5. Upon a labouring day without the sign
  6. Of your profession? Speak, what trade art thou?
  1. First Commoner
  2. Why, sir, a carpenter.
  1. MARULLUS
  2. Where is thy leather apron and thy rule?
  3. What dost thou with thy best apparel on?
  4. You, sir, what trade are you?
  1. Second Commoner
  2. Truly, sir, in respect of a fine workman, I am but,
  3. as you would say, a cobbler.
  1. MARULLUS
  2. But what trade art thou? answer me directly.
  1. Second Commoner
  2. A trade, sir, that, I hope, I may use with a safe
  3. conscience; which is, indeed, sir, a mender of bad soles.
  1. MARULLUS
  2. What trade, thou knave? thou naughty knave, what trade?
  1. Second Commoner
  2. Nay, I beseech you, sir, be not out with me: yet,
  3. if you be out, sir, I can mend you.
  1. MARULLUS
  2. What meanest thou by that? mend me, thou saucy fellow!
  1. Second Commoner
  2. Why, sir, cobble you.
  1. FLAVIUS
  2. Thou art a cobbler, art thou?
  1. Second Commoner
  2. Truly, sir, all that I live by is with the awl: I
  3. meddle with no tradesman's matters, nor women's
  4. matters, but with awl. I am, indeed, sir, a surgeon
  5. to old shoes; when they are in great danger, I
  6. recover them. As proper men as ever trod upon
  7. neat's leather have gone upon my handiwork.
  1. FLAVIUS
  2. But wherefore art not in thy shop today?
  3. Why dost thou lead these men about the streets?
  1. Second Commoner
  2. Truly, sir, to wear out their shoes, to get myself
  3. into more work. But, indeed, sir, we make holiday,
  4. to see Caesar and to rejoice in his triumph.
  1. MARULLUS
  2. Wherefore rejoice? What conquest brings he home?
  3. What tributaries follow him to Rome,
  4. To grace in captive bonds his chariot-wheels?
  5. You blocks, you stones, you worse than senseless things!
  6. O you hard hearts, you cruel men of Rome,
  7. Knew you not Pompey? Many a time and oft
  8. Have you climb'd up to walls and battlements,
  9. To towers and windows, yea, to chimney-tops,
  10. Your infants in your arms, and there have sat
  11. The livelong day, with patient expectation,
  12. To see great Pompey pass the streets of Rome:
  13. And when you saw his chariot but appear,
  14. Have you not made an universal shout,
  15. That Tiber trembled underneath her banks,
  16. To hear the replication of your sounds
  17. Made in her concave shores?
  18. And do you now put on your best attire?
  19. And do you now cull out a holiday?
  20. And do you now strew flowers in his way
  21. That comes in triumph over Pompey's blood? Be gone!
  22. Run to your houses, fall upon your knees,
  23. Pray to the gods to intermit the plague
  24. That needs must light on this ingratitude.
  1. FLAVIUS
  2. Go, go, good countrymen, and, for this fault,
  3. Assemble all the poor men of your sort;
  4. Draw them to Tiber banks, and weep your tears
  5. Into the channel, till the lowest stream
  6. Do kiss the most exalted shores of all.
  7. Exeunt all the Commoners
  8. See whether their basest metal be not moved;
  9. They vanish tongue-tied in their guiltiness.
  10. Go you down that way towards the Capitol;
  11. This way will I disrobe the images,
  12. If you do find them deck'd with ceremonies.
  1. MARULLUS
  2. May we do so?
  3. You know it is the feast of Lupercal.
  1. FLAVIUS
  2. It is no matter; let no images
  3. Be hung with Caesar's trophies. I'll about,
  4. And drive away the vulgar from the streets:
  5. So do you too, where you perceive them thick.
  6. These growing feathers pluck'd from Caesar's wing
  7. Will make him fly an ordinary pitch,
  8. Who else would soar above the view of men
  9. And keep us all in servile fearfulness.
Exeunt

SCENE II. A public place.

Flourish. Enter CAESAR; ANTONY, for the course; CALPURNIA, PORTIA, DECIUS BRUTUS, CICERO, BRUTUS, CASSIUS, and CASCA; a great crowd following, among them a Soothsayer
  1. CAESAR
  2. Calpurnia!
  1. CASCA
  2. Peace, ho! Caesar speaks.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Calpurnia!
  1. CALPURNIA
  2. Here, my lord.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Stand you directly in Antonius' way,
  3. When he doth run his course. Antonius!
  1. ANTONY
  2. Caesar, my lord?
  1. CAESAR
  2. Forget not, in your speed, Antonius,
  3. To touch Calpurnia; for our elders say,
  4. The barren, touched in this holy chase,
  5. Shake off their sterile curse.
  1. ANTONY
  2. I shall remember:
  3. When Caesar says 'do this,' it is perform'd.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Set on; and leave no ceremony out.
Flourish
  1. Soothsayer
  2. Caesar!
  1. CAESAR
  2. Ha! who calls?
  1. CASCA
  2. Bid every noise be still: peace yet again!
  1. CAESAR
  2. Who is it in the press that calls on me?
  3. I hear a tongue, shriller than all the music,
  4. Cry 'Caesar!' Speak; Caesar is turn'd to hear.
  1. Soothsayer
  2. Beware the ides of March.
  1. CAESAR
  2. What man is that?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. A soothsayer bids you beware the ides of March.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Set him before me; let me see his face.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Fellow, come from the throng; look upon Caesar.
  1. CAESAR
  2. What say'st thou to me now? speak once again.
  1. Soothsayer
  2. Beware the ides of March.
  1. CAESAR
  2. He is a dreamer; let us leave him: pass.
Sennet. Exeunt all except BRUTUS and CASSIUS
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Will you go see the order of the course?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Not I.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. I pray you, do.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. I am not gamesome: I do lack some part
  3. Of that quick spirit that is in Antony.
  4. Let me not hinder, Cassius, your desires;
  5. I'll leave you.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Brutus, I do observe you now of late:
  3. I have not from your eyes that gentleness
  4. And show of love as I was wont to have:
  5. You bear too stubborn and too strange a hand
  6. Over your friend that loves you.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Cassius,
  3. Be not deceived: if I have veil'd my look,
  4. I turn the trouble of my countenance
  5. Merely upon myself. Vexed I am
  6. Of late with passions of some difference,
  7. Conceptions only proper to myself,
  8. Which give some soil perhaps to my behaviors;
  9. But let not therefore my good friends be grieved —
  10. Among which number, Cassius, be you one —
  11. Nor construe any further my neglect,
  12. Than that poor Brutus, with himself at war,
  13. Forgets the shows of love to other men.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Then, Brutus, I have much mistook your passion;
  3. By means whereof this breast of mine hath buried
  4. Thoughts of great value, worthy cogitations.
  5. Tell me, good Brutus, can you see your face?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. No, Cassius; for the eye sees not itself,
  3. But by reflection, by some other things.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. 'Tis just:
  3. And it is very much lamented, Brutus,
  4. That you have no such mirrors as will turn
  5. Your hidden worthiness into your eye,
  6. That you might see your shadow. I have heard,
  7. Where many of the best respect in Rome,
  8. Except immortal Caesar, speaking of Brutus
  9. And groaning underneath this age's yoke,
  10. Have wish'd that noble Brutus had his eyes.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Into what dangers would you lead me, Cassius,
  3. That you would have me seek into myself
  4. For that which is not in me?
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Therefore, good Brutus, be prepared to hear:
  3. And since you know you cannot see yourself
  4. So well as by reflection, I, your glass,
  5. Will modestly discover to yourself
  6. That of yourself which you yet know not of.
  7. And be not jealous on me, gentle Brutus:
  8. Were I a common laugher, or did use
  9. To stale with ordinary oaths my love
  10. To every new protester; if you know
  11. That I do fawn on men and hug them hard
  12. And after scandal them, or if you know
  13. That I profess myself in banqueting
  14. To all the rout, then hold me dangerous.
Flourish, and shout
  1. BRUTUS
  2. What means this shouting? I do fear, the people
  3. Choose Caesar for their king.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Ay, do you fear it?
  3. Then must I think you would not have it so.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. I would not, Cassius; yet I love him well.
  3. But wherefore do you hold me here so long?
  4. What is it that you would impart to me?
  5. If it be aught toward the general good,
  6. Set honour in one eye and death i' the other,
  7. And I will look on both indifferently,
  8. For let the gods so speed me as I love
  9. The name of honour more than I fear death.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. I know that virtue to be in you, Brutus,
  3. As well as I do know your outward favour.
  4. Well, honour is the subject of my story.
  5. I cannot tell what you and other men
  6. Think of this life; but, for my single self,
  7. I had as lief not be as live to be
  8. In awe of such a thing as I myself.
  9. I was born free as Caesar; so were you:
  10. We both have fed as well, and we can both
  11. Endure the winter's cold as well as he:
  12. For once, upon a raw and gusty day,
  13. The troubled Tiber chafing with her shores,
  14. Caesar said to me 'Darest thou, Cassius, now
  15. Leap in with me into this angry flood,
  16. And swim to yonder point?' Upon the word,
  17. Accoutred as I was, I plunged in
  18. And bade him follow; so indeed he did.
  19. The torrent roar'd, and we did buffet it
  20. With lusty sinews, throwing it aside
  21. And stemming it with hearts of controversy;
  22. But ere we could arrive the point proposed,
  23. Caesar cried 'Help me, Cassius, or I sink!'
  24. I, as Aeneas, our great ancestor,
  25. Did from the flames of Troy upon his shoulder
  26. The old Anchises bear, so from the waves of Tiber
  27. Did I the tired Caesar. And this man
  28. Is now become a god, and Cassius is
  29. A wretched creature and must bend his body,
  30. If Caesar carelessly but nod on him.
  31. He had a fever when he was in Spain,
  32. And when the fit was on him, I did mark
  33. How he did shake: 'tis true, this god did shake;
  34. His coward lips did from their colour fly,
  35. And that same eye whose bend doth awe the world
  36. Did lose his lustre: I did hear him groan:
  37. Ay, and that tongue of his that bade the Romans
  38. Mark him and write his speeches in their books,
  39. Alas, it cried 'Give me some drink, Titinius,'
  40. As a sick girl. Ye gods, it doth amaze me
  41. A man of such a feeble temper should
  42. So get the start of the majestic world
  43. And bear the palm alone.
Shout. Flourish
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Another general shout!
  3. I do believe that these applauses are
  4. For some new honours that are heap'd on Caesar.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Why, man, he doth bestride the narrow world
  3. Like a Colossus, and we petty men
  4. Walk under his huge legs and peep about
  5. To find ourselves dishonourable graves.
  6. Men at some time are masters of their fates:
  7. The fault, dear Brutus, is not in our stars,
  8. But in ourselves, that we are underlings.
  9. Brutus and Caesar: what should be in that 'Caesar'?
  10. Why should that name be sounded more than yours?
  11. Write them together, yours is as fair a name;
  12. Sound them, it doth become the mouth as well;
  13. Weigh them, it is as heavy; conjure with 'em,
  14. Brutus will start a spirit as soon as Caesar.
  15. Now, in the names of all the gods at once,
  16. Upon what meat doth this our Caesar feed,
  17. That he is grown so great? Age, thou art shamed!
  18. Rome, thou hast lost the breed of noble bloods!
  19. When went there by an age, since the great flood,
  20. But it was famed with more than with one man?
  21. When could they say till now, that talk'd of Rome,
  22. That her wide walls encompass'd but one man?
  23. Now is it Rome indeed and room enough,
  24. When there is in it but one only man.
  25. O, you and I have heard our fathers say,
  26. There was a Brutus once that would have brook'd
  27. The eternal devil to keep his state in Rome
  28. As easily as a king.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. That you do love me, I am nothing jealous;
  3. What you would work me to, I have some aim:
  4. How I have thought of this and of these times,
  5. I shall recount hereafter; for this present,
  6. I would not, so with love I might entreat you,
  7. Be any further moved. What you have said
  8. I will consider; what you have to say
  9. I will with patience hear, and find a time
  10. Both meet to hear and answer such high things.
  11. Till then, my noble friend, chew upon this:
  12. Brutus had rather be a villager
  13. Than to repute himself a son of Rome
  14. Under these hard conditions as this time
  15. Is like to lay upon us.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. I am glad that my weak words
  3. Have struck but thus much show of fire from Brutus.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. The games are done and Caesar is returning.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. As they pass by, pluck Casca by the sleeve;
  3. And he will, after his sour fashion, tell you
  4. What hath proceeded worthy note to-day.
Re-enter CAESAR and his Train
  1. BRUTUS
  2. I will do so. But, look you, Cassius,
  3. The angry spot doth glow on Caesar's brow,
  4. And all the rest look like a chidden train:
  5. Calpurnia's cheek is pale; and Cicero
  6. Looks with such ferret and such fiery eyes
  7. As we have seen him in the Capitol,
  8. Being cross'd in conference by some senators.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Casca will tell us what the matter is.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Antonius!
  1. ANTONY
  2. Caesar?
  1. CAESAR
  2. Let me have men about me that are fat;
  3. Sleek-headed men and such as sleep o' nights:
  4. Yond Cassius has a lean and hungry look;
  5. He thinks too much: such men are dangerous.
  1. ANTONY
  2. Fear him not, Caesar; he's not dangerous;
  3. He is a noble Roman and well given.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Would he were fatter! But I fear him not:
  3. Yet if my name were liable to fear,
  4. I do not know the man I should avoid
  5. So soon as that spare Cassius. He reads much;
  6. He is a great observer and he looks
  7. Quite through the deeds of men: he loves no plays,
  8. As thou dost, Antony; he hears no music;
  9. Seldom he smiles, and smiles in such a sort
  10. As if he mock'd himself and scorn'd his spirit
  11. That could be moved to smile at any thing.
  12. Such men as he be never at heart's ease
  13. Whiles they behold a greater than themselves,
  14. And therefore are they very dangerous.
  15. I rather tell thee what is to be fear'd
  16. Than what I fear; for always I am Caesar.
  17. Come on my right hand, for this ear is deaf,
  18. And tell me truly what thou think'st of him.
Sennet. Exeunt CAESAR and all his Train, but CASCA
  1. CASCA
  2. You pull'd me by the cloak; would you speak with me?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Ay, Casca; tell us what hath chanced to-day,
  3. That Caesar looks so sad.
  1. CASCA
  2. Why, you were with him, were you not?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. I should not then ask Casca what had chanced.
  1. CASCA
  2. Why, there was a crown offered him: and being
  3. offered him, he put it by with the back of his hand,
  4. thus; and then the people fell a-shouting.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. What was the second noise for?
  1. CASCA
  2. Why, for that too.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. They shouted thrice: what was the last cry for?
  1. CASCA
  2. Why, for that too.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Was the crown offered him thrice?
  1. CASCA
  2. Ay, marry, was't, and he put it by thrice, every
  3. time gentler than other, and at every putting-by
  4. mine honest neighbours shouted.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Who offered him the crown?
  1. CASCA
  2. Why, Antony.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Tell us the manner of it, gentle Casca.
  1. CASCA
  2. I can as well be hanged as tell the manner of it:
  3. it was mere foolery; I did not mark it. I saw Mark
  4. Antony offer him a crown; — yet 'twas not a crown
  5. neither, 'twas one of these coronets; — and, as I told
  6. you, he put it by once: but, for all that, to my
  7. thinking, he would fain have had it. Then he
  8. offered it to him again; then he put it by again:
  9. but, to my thinking, he was very loath to lay his
  10. fingers off it. And then he offered it the third
  11. time; he put it the third time by: and still as he
  12. refused it, the rabblement hooted and clapped their
  13. chapped hands and threw up their sweaty night-caps
  14. and uttered such a deal of stinking breath because
  15. Caesar refused the crown that it had almost choked
  16. Caesar; for he swounded and fell down at it: and
  17. for mine own part, I durst not laugh, for fear of
  18. opening my lips and receiving the bad air.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. But, soft, I pray you: what, did Caesar swound?
  1. CASCA
  2. He fell down in the market-place, and foamed at
  3. mouth, and was speechless.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. 'Tis very like: he hath the failing sickness.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. No, Caesar hath it not; but you and I,
  3. And honest Casca, we have the falling sickness.
  1. CASCA
  2. I know not what you mean by that; but, I am sure,
  3. Caesar fell down. If the tag-rag people did not
  4. clap him and hiss him, according as he pleased and
  5. displeased them, as they use to do the players in
  6. the theatre, I am no true man.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. What said he when he came unto himself?
  1. CASCA
  2. Marry, before he fell down, when he perceived the
  3. common herd was glad he refused the crown, he
  4. plucked me ope his doublet and offered them his
  5. throat to cut. An I had been a man of any
  6. occupation, if I would not have taken him at a word,
  7. I would I might go to hell among the rogues. And so
  8. he fell. When he came to himself again, he said,
  9. If he had done or said any thing amiss, he desired
  10. their worships to think it was his infirmity. Three
  11. or four wenches, where I stood, cried 'Alas, good
  12. soul!' and forgave him with all their hearts: but
  13. there's no heed to be taken of them; if Caesar had
  14. stabbed their mothers, they would have done no less.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. And after that, he came, thus sad, away?
  1. CASCA
  2. Ay.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Did Cicero say any thing?
  1. CASCA
  2. Ay, he spoke Greek.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. To what effect?
  1. CASCA
  2. Nay, an I tell you that, Ill ne'er look you i' the
  3. face again: but those that understood him smiled at
  4. one another and shook their heads; but, for mine own
  5. part, it was Greek to me. I could tell you more
  6. news too: Marullus and Flavius, for pulling scarfs
  7. off Caesar's images, are put to silence. Fare you
  8. well. There was more foolery yet, if I could
  9. remember it.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Will you sup with me to-night, Casca?
  1. CASCA
  2. No, I am promised forth.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Will you dine with me to-morrow?
  1. CASCA
  2. Ay, if I be alive and your mind hold and your dinner
  3. worth the eating.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Good: I will expect you.
  1. CASCA
  2. Do so. Farewell, both.
Exit
  1. BRUTUS
  2. What a blunt fellow is this grown to be!
  3. He was quick mettle when he went to school.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. So is he now in execution
  3. Of any bold or noble enterprise,
  4. However he puts on this tardy form.
  5. This rudeness is a sauce to his good wit,
  6. Which gives men stomach to digest his words
  7. With better appetite.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. And so it is. For this time I will leave you:
  3. To-morrow, if you please to speak with me,
  4. I will come home to you; or, if you will,
  5. Come home to me, and I will wait for you.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. I will do so: till then, think of the world.
  3. Exit BRUTUS
  4. Well, Brutus, thou art noble; yet, I see,
  5. Thy honourable metal may be wrought
  6. From that it is disposed: therefore it is meet
  7. That noble minds keep ever with their likes;
  8. For who so firm that cannot be seduced?
  9. Caesar doth bear me hard; but he loves Brutus:
  10. If I were Brutus now and he were Cassius,
  11. He should not humour me. I will this night,
  12. In several hands, in at his windows throw,
  13. As if they came from several citizens,
  14. Writings all tending to the great opinion
  15. That Rome holds of his name; wherein obscurely
  16. Caesar's ambition shall be glanced at:
  17. And after this let Caesar seat him sure;
  18. For we will shake him, or worse days endure.
Exit

SCENE III. The same. A street.

Thunder and lightning. Enter from opposite sides, CASCA, with his sword drawn, and CICERO
  1. CICERO
  2. Good even, Casca: brought you Caesar home?
  3. Why are you breathless? and why stare you so?
  1. CASCA
  2. Are not you moved, when all the sway of earth
  3. Shakes like a thing unfirm? O Cicero,
  4. I have seen tempests, when the scolding winds
  5. Have rived the knotty oaks, and I have seen
  6. The ambitious ocean swell and rage and foam,
  7. To be exalted with the threatening clouds:
  8. But never till to-night, never till now,
  9. Did I go through a tempest dropping fire.
  10. Either there is a civil strife in heaven,
  11. Or else the world, too saucy with the gods,
  12. Incenses them to send destruction.
  1. CICERO
  2. Why, saw you any thing more wonderful?
  1. CASCA
  2. A common slave — you know him well by sight —
  3. Held up his left hand, which did flame and burn
  4. Like twenty torches join'd, and yet his hand,
  5. Not sensible of fire, remain'd unscorch'd.
  6. Besides — I ha' not since put up my sword —
  7. Against the Capitol I met a lion,
  8. Who glared upon me, and went surly by,
  9. Without annoying me: and there were drawn
  10. Upon a heap a hundred ghastly women,
  11. Transformed with their fear; who swore they saw
  12. Men all in fire walk up and down the streets.
  13. And yesterday the bird of night did sit
  14. Even at noon-day upon the market-place,
  15. Hooting and shrieking. When these prodigies
  16. Do so conjointly meet, let not men say
  17. 'These are their reasons; they are natural;'
  18. For, I believe, they are portentous things
  19. Unto the climate that they point upon.
  1. CICERO
  2. Indeed, it is a strange-disposed time:
  3. But men may construe things after their fashion,
  4. Clean from the purpose of the things themselves.
  5. Come Caesar to the Capitol to-morrow?
  1. CASCA
  2. He doth; for he did bid Antonius
  3. Send word to you he would be there to-morrow.
  1. CICERO
  2. Good night then, Casca: this disturbed sky
  3. Is not to walk in.
  1. CASCA
  2. Farewell, Cicero.
Exit CICERO
Enter CASSIUS
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Who's there?
  1. CASCA
  2. A Roman.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Casca, by your voice.
  1. CASCA
  2. Your ear is good. Cassius, what night is this!
  1. CASSIUS
  2. A very pleasing night to honest men.
  1. CASCA
  2. Who ever knew the heavens menace so?
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Those that have known the earth so full of faults.
  3. For my part, I have walk'd about the streets,
  4. Submitting me unto the perilous night,
  5. And, thus unbraced, Casca, as you see,
  6. Have bared my bosom to the thunder-stone;
  7. And when the cross blue lightning seem'd to open
  8. The breast of heaven, I did present myself
  9. Even in the aim and very flash of it.
  1. CASCA
  2. But wherefore did you so much tempt the heavens?
  3. It is the part of men to fear and tremble,
  4. When the most mighty gods by tokens send
  5. Such dreadful heralds to astonish us.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. You are dull, Casca, and those sparks of life
  3. That should be in a Roman you do want,
  4. Or else you use not. You look pale and gaze
  5. And put on fear and cast yourself in wonder,
  6. To see the strange impatience of the heavens:
  7. But if you would consider the true cause
  8. Why all these fires, why all these gliding ghosts,
  9. Why birds and beasts from quality and kind,
  10. Why old men fool and children calculate,
  11. Why all these things change from their ordinance
  12. Their natures and preformed faculties
  13. To monstrous quality — why, you shall find
  14. That heaven hath infused them with these spirits,
  15. To make them instruments of fear and warning
  16. Unto some monstrous state.
  17. Now could I, Casca, name to thee a man
  18. Most like this dreadful night,
  19. That thunders, lightens, opens graves, and roars
  20. As doth the lion in the Capitol,
  21. A man no mightier than thyself or me
  22. In personal action, yet prodigious grown
  23. And fearful, as these strange eruptions are.
  1. CASCA
  2. 'Tis Caesar that you mean; is it not, Cassius?
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Let it be who it is: for Romans now
  3. Have thews and limbs like to their ancestors;
  4. But, woe the while! our fathers' minds are dead,
  5. And we are govern'd with our mothers' spirits;
  6. Our yoke and sufferance show us womanish.
  1. CASCA
  2. Indeed, they say the senators tomorrow
  3. Mean to establish Caesar as a king;
  4. And he shall wear his crown by sea and land,
  5. In every place, save here in Italy.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. I know where I will wear this dagger then;
  3. Cassius from bondage will deliver Cassius:
  4. Therein, ye gods, you make the weak most strong;
  5. Therein, ye gods, you tyrants do defeat:
  6. Nor stony tower, nor walls of beaten brass,
  7. Nor airless dungeon, nor strong links of iron,
  8. Can be retentive to the strength of spirit;
  9. But life, being weary of these worldly bars,
  10. Never lacks power to dismiss itself.
  11. If I know this, know all the world besides,
  12. That part of tyranny that I do bear
  13. I can shake off at pleasure.
Thunder still
  1. CASCA
  2. So can I:
  3. So every bondman in his own hand bears
  4. The power to cancel his captivity.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. And why should Caesar be a tyrant then?
  3. Poor man! I know he would not be a wolf,
  4. But that he sees the Romans are but sheep:
  5. He were no lion, were not Romans hinds.
  6. Those that with haste will make a mighty fire
  7. Begin it with weak straws: what trash is Rome,
  8. What rubbish and what offal, when it serves
  9. For the base matter to illuminate
  10. So vile a thing as Caesar! But, O grief,
  11. Where hast thou led me? I perhaps speak this
  12. Before a willing bondman; then I know
  13. My answer must be made. But I am arm'd,
  14. And dangers are to me indifferent.
  1. CASCA
  2. You speak to Casca, and to such a man
  3. That is no fleering tell-tale. Hold, my hand:
  4. Be factious for redress of all these griefs,
  5. And I will set this foot of mine as far
  6. As who goes farthest.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. There's a bargain made.
  3. Now know you, Casca, I have moved already
  4. Some certain of the noblest-minded Romans
  5. To undergo with me an enterprise
  6. Of honourable-dangerous consequence;
  7. And I do know, by this, they stay for me
  8. In Pompey's porch: for now, this fearful night,
  9. There is no stir or walking in the streets;
  10. And the complexion of the element
  11. In favour's like the work we have in hand,
  12. Most bloody, fiery, and most terrible.
  1. CASCA
  2. Stand close awhile, for here comes one in haste.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. 'Tis Cinna; I do know him by his gait;
  3. He is a friend.
  4. Enter CINNA
  5. Cinna, where haste you so?
  1. CINNA
  2. To find out you. Who's that? Metellus Cimber?
  1. CASSIUS
  2. No, it is Casca; one incorporate
  3. To our attempts. Am I not stay'd for, Cinna?
  1. CINNA
  2. I am glad on 't. What a fearful night is this!
  3. There's two or three of us have seen strange sights.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Am I not stay'd for? tell me.
  1. CINNA
  2. Yes, you are.
  3. O Cassius, if you could
  4. But win the noble Brutus to our party —
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Be you content: good Cinna, take this paper,
  3. And look you lay it in the praetor's chair,
  4. Where Brutus may but find it; and throw this
  5. In at his window; set this up with wax
  6. Upon old Brutus' statue: all this done,
  7. Repair to Pompey's porch, where you shall find us.
  8. Is Decius Brutus and Trebonius there?
  1. CINNA
  2. All but Metellus Cimber; and he's gone
  3. To seek you at your house. Well, I will hie,
  4. And so bestow these papers as you bade me.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. That done, repair to Pompey's theatre.
  3. Exit CINNA
  4. Come, Casca, you and I will yet ere day
  5. See Brutus at his house: three parts of him
  6. Is ours already, and the man entire
  7. Upon the next encounter yields him ours.
  1. CASCA
  2. O, he sits high in all the people's hearts:
  3. And that which would appear offence in us,
  4. His countenance, like richest alchemy,
  5. Will change to virtue and to worthiness.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Him and his worth and our great need of him
  3. You have right well conceited. Let us go,
  4. For it is after midnight; and ere day
  5. We will awake him and be sure of him.
Exeunt

ACT II

SCENE I. Rome. BRUTUS's orchard.

Enter BRUTUS
  1. BRUTUS
  2. What, Lucius, ho!
  3. I cannot, by the progress of the stars,
  4. Give guess how near to day. Lucius, I say!
  5. I would it were my fault to sleep so soundly.
  6. When, Lucius, when? awake, I say! what, Lucius!
Enter LUCIUS
  1. LUCIUS
  2. Call'd you, my lord?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Get me a taper in my study, Lucius:
  3. When it is lighted, come and call me here.
  1. LUCIUS
  2. I will, my lord.
Exit
  1. BRUTUS
  2. It must be by his death: and for my part,
  3. I know no personal cause to spurn at him,
  4. But for the general. He would be crown'd:
  5. How that might change his nature, there's the question.
  6. It is the bright day that brings forth the adder;
  7. And that craves wary walking. Crown him? — that; —
  8. And then, I grant, we put a sting in him,
  9. That at his will he may do danger with.
  10. The abuse of greatness is, when it disjoins
  11. Remorse from power: and, to speak truth of Caesar,
  12. I have not known when his affections sway'd
  13. More than his reason. But 'tis a common proof,
  14. That lowliness is young ambition's ladder,
  15. Whereto the climber-upward turns his face;
  16. But when he once attains the upmost round.
  17. He then unto the ladder turns his back,
  18. Looks in the clouds, scorning the base degrees
  19. By which he did ascend. So Caesar may.
  20. Then, lest he may, prevent. And, since the quarrel
  21. Will bear no colour for the thing he is,
  22. Fashion it thus; that what he is, augmented,
  23. Would run to these and these extremities:
  24. And therefore think him as a serpent's egg
  25. Which, hatch'd, would, as his kind, grow mischievous,
  26. And kill him in the shell.
Re-enter LUCIUS
  1. LUCIUS
  2. The taper burneth in your closet, sir.
  3. Searching the window for a flint, I found
  4. This paper, thus seal'd up; and, I am sure,
  5. It did not lie there when I went to bed.
Gives him the letter
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Get you to bed again; it is not day.
  3. Is not to-morrow, boy, the ides of March?
  1. LUCIUS
  2. I know not, sir.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Look in the calendar, and bring me word.
  1. LUCIUS
  2. I will, sir.
Exit
  1. BRUTUS

  3. Give so much light that I may read by them.
  4. Opens the letter and reads
  5. 'Brutus, thou sleep'st: awake, and see thyself.
  6. Shall Rome, etc.. Speak, strike, redress!
  7. Brutus, thou sleep'st: awake!'

  9. Where I have took them up.
  10. 'Shall Rome, &c.' Thus must I piece it out:
  11. Shall Rome stand under one man's awe? What, Rome?
  12. My ancestors did from the streets of Rome
  13. The Tarquin drive, when he was call'd a king.
  14. 'Speak, strike, redress!' Am I entreated
  15. To speak and strike? O Rome, I make thee promise:
  16. If the redress will follow, thou receivest
  17. Thy full petition at the hand of Brutus!
Re-enter LUCIUS
  1. LUCIUS
  2. Sir, March is wasted fourteen days.
Knocking within
  1. BRUTUS
  2. 'Tis good. Go to the gate; somebody knocks.
  3. Exit LUCIUS
  4. Since Cassius first did whet me against Caesar,
  5. I have not slept.
  6. Between the acting of a dreadful thing
  7. And the first motion, all the interim is
  8. Like a phantasma, or a hideous dream:
  9. The Genius and the mortal instruments
  10. Are then in council; and the state of man,
  11. Like to a little kingdom, suffers then
  12. The nature of an insurrection.
Re-enter LUCIUS
  1. LUCIUS
  2. Sir, 'tis your brother Cassius at the door,
  3. Who doth desire to see you.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Is he alone?
  1. LUCIUS
  2. No, sir, there are moe with him.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Do you know them?
  1. LUCIUS
  2. No, sir; their hats are pluck'd about their ears,
  3. And half their faces buried in their cloaks,

  5. By any mark of favour.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Let 'em enter.
  3. Exit LUCIUS

  5. Shamest thou to show thy dangerous brow by night,
  6. When evils are most free? O, then by day
  7. Where wilt thou find a cavern dark enough
  8. To mask thy monstrous visage? Seek none, conspiracy;
  9. Hide it in smiles and affability:
  10. For if thou path, thy native semblance on,
  11. Not Erebus itself were dim enough
  12. To hide thee from prevention.

  1. BRUTUS

  3. Know I these men that come along with you?
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Yes, every man of them, and no man here
  3. But honours you; and every one doth wish
  4. You had but that opinion of yourself
  5. Which every noble Roman bears of you.

  1. BRUTUS

  1. CASSIUS
  2. This, Decius Brutus.
  1. BRUTUS

  1. CASSIUS
  2. This, Casca; this, Cinna; and this, Metellus Cimber.

  2. They are all welcome.

  4. Betwixt your eyes and night?
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Shall I entreat a word?
BRUTUS and CASSIUS whisper

  1. CASCA
  2. No.
  1. CINNA

  2. You shall confess that you are both deceived.

  4. Which is a great way growing on the south,
  5. Weighing the youthful season of the year.
  6. Some two months hence up higher toward the north
  7. He first presents his fire; and the high east
  8. Stands, as the Capitol, directly here.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Give me your hands all over, one by one.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. And let us swear our resolution.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. No, not an oath: if not the face of men,
  3. The sufferance of our souls, the time's abuse —
  4. If these be motives weak, break off betimes,
  5. And every man hence to his idle bed;
  6. So let high-sighted tyranny range on,
  7. Till each man drop by lottery. But if these,
  8. As I am sure they do, bear fire enough
  9. To kindle cowards and to steel with valour
  10. The melting spirits of women, then, countrymen,
  11. What need we any spur but our own cause,
  12. To prick us to redress? what other bond
  13. Than secret Romans, that have spoke the word,
  14. And will not palter? and what other oath
  15. Than honesty to honesty engaged,
  16. That this shall be, or we will fall for it?
  17. Swear priests and cowards and men cautelous,
  18. Old feeble carrions and such suffering souls
  19. That welcome wrongs; unto bad causes swear
  20. Such creatures as men doubt; but do not stain
  21. The even virtue of our enterprise,
  22. Nor the insuppressive mettle of our spirits,
  23. To think that or our cause or our performance
  24. Did need an oath; when every drop of blood
  25. That every Roman bears, and nobly bears,
  26. Is guilty of a several bastardy,
  27. If he do break the smallest particle
  28. Of any promise that hath pass'd from him.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. But what of Cicero? shall we sound him?
  3. I think he will stand very strong with us.
  1. CASCA
  2. Let us not leave him out.
  1. CINNA
  2. No, by no means.
  1. METELLUS CIMBER
  2. O, let us have him, for his silver hairs
  3. Will purchase us a good opinion
  4. And buy men's voices to commend our deeds:
  5. It shall be said, his judgment ruled our hands;
  6. Our youths and wildness shall no whit appear,
  7. But all be buried in his gravity.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. O, name him not: let us not break with him;
  3. For he will never follow any thing
  4. That other men begin.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Then leave him out.
  1. CASCA
  2. Indeed he is not fit.
  1. DECIUS BRUTUS
  2. Shall no man else be touch'd but only Caesar?
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Decius, well urged: I think it is not meet,
  3. Mark Antony, so well beloved of Caesar,
  4. Should outlive Caesar: we shall find of him
  5. A shrewd contriver; and, you know, his means,
  6. If he improve them, may well stretch so far
  7. As to annoy us all: which to prevent,
  8. Let Antony and Caesar fall together.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Our course will seem too bloody, Caius Cassius,
  3. To cut the head off and then hack the limbs,
  4. Like wrath in death and envy afterwards;
  5. For Antony is but a limb of Caesar:
  6. Let us be sacrificers, but not butchers, Caius.
  7. We all stand up against the spirit of Caesar;
  8. And in the spirit of men there is no blood:
  9. O, that we then could come by Caesar's spirit,
  10. And not dismember Caesar! But, alas,
  11. Caesar must bleed for it! And, gentle friends,
  12. Let's kill him boldly, but not wrathfully;
  13. Let's carve him as a dish fit for the gods,
  14. Not hew him as a carcass fit for hounds:
  15. And let our hearts, as subtle masters do,
  16. Stir up their servants to an act of rage,
  17. And after seem to chide 'em. This shall make
  18. Our purpose necessary and not envious:
  19. Which so appearing to the common eyes,
  20. We shall be call'd purgers, not murderers.
  21. And for Mark Antony, think not of him;
  22. For he can do no more than Caesar's arm
  23. When Caesar's head is off.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Yet I fear him;
  3. For in the ingrafted love he bears to Caesar —
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Alas, good Cassius, do not think of him:
  3. If he love Caesar, all that he can do
  4. Is to himself, take thought and die for Caesar:
  5. And that were much he should; for he is given
  6. To sports, to wildness and much company.
  1. TREBONIUS
  2. There is no fear in him; let him not die;
  3. For he will live, and laugh at this hereafter.

  1. BRUTUS
  2. Peace! count the clock.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. The clock hath stricken three.
  1. TREBONIUS
  2. 'Tis time to part.

  1. CINNA

  2. Now, good Metellus, go along by him:

  4. Send him but hither, and I'll fashion him.

  2. The morning comes upon 's: we'll leave you, Brutus.

  4. What you have said, and show yourselves true Romans.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Good gentlemen, look fresh and merrily;

  4. But bear it as our Roman actors do,

  10. Thou hast no figures nor no fantasies,

  12. Therefore thou sleep'st so sound.

  1. PORTIA
  2. Brutus, my lord!
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Portia, what mean you? wherefore rise you now?
  3. It is not for your health thus to commit
  4. Your weak condition to the raw cold morning.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Nor for yours neither. You've ungently, Brutus,
  3. Stole from my bed: and yesternight, at supper,
  4. You suddenly arose, and walk'd about,
  5. Musing and sighing, with your arms across,
  6. And when I ask'd you what the matter was,
  7. You stared upon me with ungentle looks;
  8. I urged you further; then you scratch'd your head,
  9. And too impatiently stamp'd with your foot;
  10. Yet I insisted, yet you answer'd not,
  11. But, with an angry wafture of your hand,
  12. Gave sign for me to leave you: so I did;
  13. Fearing to strengthen that impatience
  14. Which seem'd too much enkindled, and withal
  15. Hoping it was but an effect of humour,
  16. Which sometime hath his hour with every man.
  17. It will not let you eat, nor talk, nor sleep,
  18. And could it work so much upon your shape
  19. As it hath much prevail'd on your condition,
  20. I should not know you, Brutus. Dear my lord,
  21. Make me acquainted with your cause of grief.

  2. I am not well in health, and that is all.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Brutus is wise, and, were he not in health,

  2. Why, so I do. Good Portia, go to bed.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Is Brutus sick? and is it physical
  3. To walk unbraced and suck up the humours
  4. Of the dank morning? What, is Brutus sick,
  5. And will he steal out of his wholesome bed,
  6. To dare the vile contagion of the night
  7. And tempt the rheumy and unpurged air
  8. To add unto his sickness? No, my Brutus;
  9. You have some sick offence within your mind,
  10. Which, by the right and virtue of my place,
  11. I ought to know of: and, upon my knees,
  12. I charm you, by my once-commended beauty,
  13. By all your vows of love and that great vow
  14. Which did incorporate and make us one,
  15. That you unfold to me, yourself, your half,
  16. Why you are heavy, and what men to-night
  17. Have had to resort to you: for here have been
  18. Some six or seven, who did hide their faces
  19. Even from darkness.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Kneel not, gentle Portia.
  1. PORTIA
  2. I should not need, if you were gentle Brutus.

  4. Is it excepted I should know no secrets
  5. That appertain to you? Am I yourself
  6. But, as it were, in sort or limitation,
  7. To keep with you at meals, comfort your bed,
  8. And talk to you sometimes? Dwell I but in the suburbs
  9. Of your good pleasure? If it be no more,
  10. Portia is Brutus' harlot, not his wife.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. You are my true and honourable wife,
  3. As dear to me as are the ruddy drops
  4. That visit my sad heart
  1. PORTIA
  2. If this were true, then should I know this secret.
  3. I grant I am a woman; but withal
  4. A woman that Lord Brutus took to wife:
  5. I grant I am a woman; but withal
  6. A woman well-reputed, Cato's daughter.
  7. Think you I am no stronger than my sex,
  8. Being so father'd and so husbanded?
  9. Tell me your counsels, I will not disclose 'em:
  10. I have made strong proof of my constancy,
  11. Giving myself a voluntary wound
  12. Here, in the thigh: can I bear that with patience.
  13. And not my husband's secrets?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. O ye gods,
  3. Render me worthy of this noble wife!
  4. Knocking within
  5. Hark, hark! one knocks: Portia, go in awhile;
  6. And by and by thy bosom shall partake
  7. The secrets of my heart.
  8. All my engagements I will construe to thee,
  9. All the charactery of my sad brows:
  10. Leave me with haste.
  11. Exit PORTIA
  12. Lucius, who's that knocks?

  1. LUCIUS
  2. He is a sick man that would speak with you.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Caius Ligarius, that Metellus spake of.

  1. LIGARIUS
  2. Vouchsafe good morrow from a feeble tongue.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. O, what a time have you chose out, brave Caius,

  1. LIGARIUS
  2. I am not sick, if Brutus have in hand

  1. BRUTUS
  2. Such an exploit have I in hand, Ligarius,

  1. LIGARIUS
  2. By all the gods that Romans bow before,

  4. Brave son, derived from honourable loins!

  2. A piece of work that will make sick men whole.
  1. LIGARIUS
  2. But are not some whole that we must make sick?
  1. BRUTUS

  4. To whom it must be done.
  1. LIGARIUS

  3. And with a heart new-fired I follow you,

  2. Nor heaven nor earth have been at peace to-night:
  3. Thrice hath Calpurnia in her sleep cried out,
  4. 'Help, ho! they murder Caesar!' Who's within?

  1. Servant
  2. My lord?
  1. CAESAR
  2. Go bid the priests do present sacrifice

  1. Servant
  2. I will, my lord.
Exit
Enter CALPURNIA
  1. CALPURNIA
  2. What mean you, Caesar? think you to walk forth?
  3. You shall not stir out of your house to-day.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Caesar shall forth: the things that threaten'd me
  3. Ne'er look'd but on my back; when they shall see
  4. The face of Caesar, they are vanished.
  1. CALPURNIA
  2. Caesar, I never stood on ceremonies,
  3. Yet now they fright me. There is one within,
  4. Besides the things that we have heard and seen,
  5. Recounts most horrid sights seen by the watch.
  6. A lioness hath whelped in the streets;
  7. And graves have yawn'd, and yielded up their dead;
  8. Fierce fiery warriors fought upon the clouds,
  9. In ranks and squadrons and right form of war,
  10. Which drizzled blood upon the Capitol;
  11. The noise of battle hurtled in the air,
  12. Horses did neigh, and dying men did groan,
  13. And ghosts did shriek and squeal about the streets.
  14. O Caesar! these things are beyond all use,
  15. And I do fear them.
  1. CAESAR
  2. What can be avoided
  3. Whose end is purposed by the mighty gods?
  4. Yet Caesar shall go forth; for these predictions
  5. Are to the world in general as to Caesar.
  1. CALPURNIA
  2. When beggars die, there are no comets seen;
  3. The heavens themselves blaze forth the death of princes.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Cowards die many times before their deaths;
  3. The valiant never taste of death but once.
  4. Of all the wonders that I yet have heard.
  5. It seems to me most strange that men should fear;
  6. Seeing that death, a necessary end,
  7. Will come when it will come.
  8. Re-enter Servant
  9. What say the augurers?
  1. Servant
  2. They would not have you to stir forth to-day.
  3. Plucking the entrails of an offering forth,
  4. They could not find a heart within the beast.
  1. CAESAR
  2. The gods do this in shame of cowardice:
  3. Caesar should be a beast without a heart,
  4. If he should stay at home to-day for fear.
  5. No, Caesar shall not: danger knows full well
  6. That Caesar is more dangerous than he:
  7. We are two lions litter'd in one day,
  8. And I the elder and more terrible:
  9. And Caesar shall go forth.
  1. CALPURNIA
  2. Alas, my lord,
  3. Your wisdom is consumed in confidence.
  4. Do not go forth to-day: call it my fear
  5. That keeps you in the house, and not your own.
  6. We'll send Mark Antony to the senate-house:
  7. And he shall say you are not well to-day:
  8. Let me, upon my knee, prevail in this.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Mark Antony shall say I am not well,
  3. And, for thy humour, I will stay at home.
  4. Enter DECIUS BRUTUS
  5. Here's Decius Brutus, he shall tell them so.
  1. DECIUS BRUTUS
  2. Caesar, all hail! good morrow, worthy Caesar:
  3. I come to fetch you to the senate-house.
  1. CAESAR
  2. And you are come in very happy time,
  3. To bear my greeting to the senators
  4. And tell them that I will not come to-day:
  5. Cannot, is false, and that I dare not, falser:
  6. I will not come to-day: tell them so, Decius.
  1. CALPURNIA
  2. Say he is sick.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Shall Caesar send a lie?
  3. Have I in conquest stretch'd mine arm so far,
  4. To be afraid to tell graybeards the truth?
  5. Decius, go tell them Caesar will not come.
  1. DECIUS BRUTUS
  2. Most mighty Caesar, let me know some cause,
  3. Lest I be laugh'd at when I tell them so.
  1. CAESAR
  2. The cause is in my will: I will not come;
  3. That is enough to satisfy the senate.
  4. But for your private satisfaction,
  5. Because I love you, I will let you know:
  6. Calpurnia here, my wife, stays me at home:
  7. She dreamt to-night she saw my statua,
  8. Which, like a fountain with an hundred spouts,
  9. Did run pure blood: and many lusty Romans
  10. Came smiling, and did bathe their hands in it:
  11. And these does she apply for warnings, and portents,
  12. And evils imminent; and on her knee
  13. Hath begg'd that I will stay at home to-day.
  1. DECIUS BRUTUS
  2. This dream is all amiss interpreted;
  3. It was a vision fair and fortunate:
  4. Your statue spouting blood in many pipes,
  5. In which so many smiling Romans bathed,
  6. Signifies that from you great Rome shall suck
  7. Reviving blood, and that great men shall press
  8. For tinctures, stains, relics and cognizance.
  9. This by Calpurnia's dream is signified.
  1. CAESAR
  2. And this way have you well expounded it.
  1. DECIUS BRUTUS
  2. I have, when you have heard what I can say:
  3. And know it now: the senate have concluded
  4. To give this day a crown to mighty Caesar.
  5. If you shall send them word you will not come,
  6. Their minds may change. Besides, it were a mock
  7. Apt to be render'd, for some one to say
  8. 'Break up the senate till another time,
  9. When Caesar's wife shall meet with better dreams.'
  10. If Caesar hide himself, shall they not whisper
  11. 'Lo, Caesar is afraid'?
  12. Pardon me, Caesar; for my dear dear love
  13. To our proceeding bids me tell you this;
  14. And reason to my love is liable.
  1. CAESAR
  2. How foolish do your fears seem now, Calpurnia!
  3. I am ashamed I did yield to them.
  4. Give me my robe, for I will go.
  5. Enter PUBLIUS, BRUTUS, LIGARIUS, METELLUS, CASCA, TREBONIUS, and CINNA
  6. And look where Publius is come to fetch me.
  1. PUBLIUS
  2. Good morrow, Caesar.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Welcome, Publius.
  3. What, Brutus, are you stirr'd so early too?
  4. Good morrow, Casca. Caius Ligarius,
  5. Caesar was ne'er so much your enemy
  6. As that same ague which hath made you lean.
  7. What is 't o'clock?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Caesar, 'tis strucken eight.
  1. CAESAR
  2. I thank you for your pains and courtesy.
  3. Enter ANTONY
  4. See! Antony, that revels long o' nights,
  5. Is notwithstanding up. Good morrow, Antony.
  1. ANTONY
  2. So to most noble Caesar.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Bid them prepare within:
  3. I am to blame to be thus waited for.
  4. Now, Cinna: now, Metellus: what, Trebonius!
  5. I have an hour's talk in store for you;
  6. Remember that you call on me to-day:
  7. Be near me, that I may remember you.
  1. TREBONIUS
  2. Caesar, I will:
  3. Aside
  4. and so near will I be,
  5. That your best friends shall wish I had been further.
  1. CAESAR
  2. Good friends, go in, and taste some wine with me;
  3. And we, like friends, will straightway go together.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Aside That every like is not the same, O Caesar,

ACT III

  1. METELLUS CIMBER
  2. Most high, most mighty, and most puissant Caesar,

  4. An humble heart —
Kneeling

  2. I must prevent thee, Cimber.

  4. Might fire the blood of ordinary men,
  5. And turn pre-ordinance and first decree
  6. Into the law of children. Be not fond,
  7. To think that Caesar bears such rebel blood
  8. That will be thaw'd from the true quality
  9. With that which melteth fools; I mean, sweet words,
  10. Low-crooked court'sies and base spaniel-fawning.
  11. Thy brother by decree is banished:
  12. If thou dost bend and pray and fawn for him,
  13. I spurn thee like a cur out of my way.
  14. Know, Caesar doth not wrong, nor without cause
  15. Will he be satisfied.
  1. METELLUS CIMBER
  2. Is there no voice more worthy than my own
  3. To sound more sweetly in great Caesar's ear
  4. For the repealing of my banish'd brother?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. I kiss thy hand, but not in flattery, Caesar;
  3. Desiring thee that Publius Cimber may
  4. Have an immediate freedom of repeal.
  1. CAESAR
  2. What, Brutus!
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Pardon, Caesar; Caesar, pardon:
  3. As low as to thy foot doth Cassius fall,
  4. To beg enfranchisement for Publius Cimber.

  2. I could be well moved, if I were as you:
  3. If I could pray to move, prayers would move me:
  4. But I am constant as the northern star,
  5. Of whose true-fix'd and resting quality
  6. There is no fellow in the firmament.
  7. The skies are painted with unnumber'd sparks,
  8. They are all fire and every one doth shine,
  9. But there's but one in all doth hold his place:
  10. So in the world; 'tis furnish'd well with men,
  11. And men are flesh and blood, and apprehensive;
  12. Yet in the number I do know but one
  13. That unassailable holds on his rank,
  14. Unshaked of motion: and that I am he,
  15. Let me a little show it, even in this;
  16. That I was constant Cimber should be banish'd,
  17. And constant do remain to keep him so.

  2. O Caesar —
  1. CAESAR
  2. Hence! wilt thou lift up Olympus?
  1. DECIUS BRUTUS
  2. Great Caesar —

  2. Doth not Brutus bootless kneel?

  1. CASCA
  2. Go to the pulpit, Brutus.

  1. BRUTUS
  2. Where's Publius?
  1. CINNA

  1. BRUTUS
  2. Talk not of standing. Publius, good cheer;

  1. CASSIUS
  2. And leave us, Publius; lest that the people,

  2. Do so: and let no man abide this deed,

  2. Where is Antony?

  2. Fates, we will know your pleasures:

  4. And drawing days out, that men stand upon.

  2. Why, he that cuts off twenty years of life

  8. And, waving our red weapons o'er our heads,
  9. Let's all cry 'Peace, freedom and liberty!'

  2. Stoop, then, and wash. How many ages hence
  3. Shall this our lofty scene be acted over
  4. In states unborn and accents yet unknown!
  1. BRUTUS
  2. How many times shall Caesar bleed in sport,
  3. That now on Pompey's basis lies along
  4. No worthier than the dust!
  1. CASSIUS
  2. So oft as that shall be,
  3. So often shall the knot of us be call'd
  4. The men that gave their country liberty.
  1. DECIUS BRUTUS
  2. What, shall we forth?
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Ay, every man away:
  3. Brutus shall lead; and we will grace his heels
  4. With the most boldest and best hearts of Rome.
Enter a Servant
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Soft! who comes here? A friend of Antony's.
  1. Servant
  2. Thus, Brutus, did my master bid me kneel:
  3. Thus did Mark Antony bid me fall down;
  4. And, being prostrate, thus he bade me say:
  5. Brutus is noble, wise, valiant, and honest;
  6. Caesar was mighty, bold, royal, and loving:
  7. Say I love Brutus, and I honour him;
  8. Say I fear'd Caesar, honour'd him and loved him.
  9. If Brutus will vouchsafe that Antony
  10. May safely come to him, and be resolved
  11. How Caesar hath deserved to lie in death,
  12. Mark Antony shall not love Caesar dead
  13. So well as Brutus living; but will follow
  14. The fortunes and affairs of noble Brutus
  15. Thorough the hazards of this untrod state
  16. With all true faith. So says my master Antony.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Thy master is a wise and valiant Roman;
  3. I never thought him worse.
  4. Tell him, so please him come unto this place,
  5. He shall be satisfied; and, by my honour,
  6. Depart untouch'd.
  1. Servant
  2. I'll fetch him presently.
Exit
  1. BRUTUS
  2. I know that we shall have him well to friend.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. I wish we may: but yet have I a mind
  3. That fears him much; and my misgiving still
  4. Falls shrewdly to the purpose.

  2. But here comes Antony.

  2. O mighty Caesar! dost thou lie so low?

  6. Who else must be let blood, who else is rank:
  7. If I myself, there is no hour so fit
  8. As Caesar's death hour, nor no instrument
  9. Of half that worth as those your swords, made rich
  10. With the most noble blood of all this world.
  11. I do beseech ye, if you bear me hard,
  12. Now, whilst your purpled hands do reek and smoke,
  13. Fulfil your pleasure. Live a thousand years,
  14. I shall not find myself so apt to die:
  15. No place will please me so, no mean of death,
  16. As here by Caesar, and by you cut off,
  17. The choice and master spirits of this age.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. O Antony, beg not your death of us.
  3. Though now we must appear bloody and cruel,
  4. As, by our hands and this our present act,
  5. You see we do, yet see you but our hands
  6. And this the bleeding business they have done:
  7. Our hearts you see not; they are pitiful;
  8. And pity to the general wrong of Rome —
  9. As fire drives out fire, so pity pity —
  10. Hath done this deed on Caesar. For your part,
  11. To you our swords have leaden points, Mark Antony:
  12. Our arms, in strength of malice, and our hearts
  13. Of brothers' temper, do receive you in
  14. With all kind love, good thoughts, and reverence.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Your voice shall be as strong as any man's
  3. In the disposing of new dignities.
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Only be patient till we have appeased
  3. The multitude, beside themselves with fear,
  4. And then we will deliver you the cause,
  5. Why I, that did love Caesar when I struck him,
  6. Have thus proceeded.
  1. ANTONY
  2. I doubt not of your wisdom.
  3. Let each man render me his bloody hand:
  4. First, Marcus Brutus, will I shake with you;
  5. Next, Caius Cassius, do I take your hand;
  6. Now, Decius Brutus, yours: now yours, Metellus;
  7. Yours, Cinna; and, my valiant Casca, yours;
  8. Though last, not last in love, yours, good Trebonius.

  2. Believe not so.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. I but believe it partly;

  2. Even so, Lucilius.

  6. Let's reason with the worst that may befall.
  7. If we do lose this battle, then is this
  8. The very last time we shall speak together:
  9. What are you then determined to do?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. Even by the rule of that philosophy
  3. By which I did blame Cato for the death
  4. Which he did give himself, I know not how,
  5. But I do find it cowardly and vile,
  6. For fear of what might fall, so to prevent
  7. The time of life: arming myself with patience
  8. To stay the providence of some high powers
  9. That govern us below.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. Then, if we lose this battle,
  3. You are contented to be led in triumph
  4. Thorough the streets of Rome?
  1. BRUTUS
  2. No, Cassius, no: think not, thou noble Roman,
  3. That ever Brutus will go bound to Rome;
  4. He bears too great a mind. But this same day
  5. Must end that work the ides of March begun;
  6. And whether we shall meet again I know not.

  10. If not, why then, this parting was well made.
  1. CASSIUS
  2. For ever, and for ever, farewell, Brutus!

  2. Why, then, lead on. O, that a man might know

  1. TITINIUS
  2. O Cassius, Brutus gave the word too early;

  2. Fly further off, my lord, fly further off;
