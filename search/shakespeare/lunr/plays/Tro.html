ACT I

SCENE I. Troy. Before Priam's palace.

Enter TROILUS armed, and PANDARUS
  1. TROILUS
  2. Call here my varlet; I'll unarm again:
  3. Why should I war without the walls of Troy,
  4. That find such cruel battle here within?
  5. Each Trojan that is master of his heart,
  6. Let him to field; Troilus, alas! hath none.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Will this gear ne'er be mended?
  1. TROILUS
  2. The Greeks are strong and skilful to their strength,
  3. Fierce to their skill and to their fierceness valiant;
  4. But I am weaker than a woman's tear,
  5. Tamer than sleep, fonder than ignorance,
  6. Less valiant than the virgin in the night
  7. And skilless as unpractised infancy.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Well, I have told you enough of this: for my part,
  3. I'll not meddle nor make no further. He that will
  4. have a cake out of the wheat must needs tarry the grinding.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Have I not tarried?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Ay, the grinding; but you must tarry
  3. the bolting.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Have I not tarried?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Ay, the bolting, but you must tarry the leavening.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Still have I tarried.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Ay, to the leavening; but here's yet in the word
  3. 'hereafter' the kneading, the making of the cake, the
  4. heating of the oven and the baking; nay, you must
  5. stay the cooling too, or you may chance to burn your lips.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Patience herself, what goddess e'er she be,
  3. Doth lesser blench at sufferance than I do.
  4. At Priam's royal table do I sit;
  5. And when fair Cressid comes into my thoughts —
  6. So, traitor! 'When she comes!' When is she thence?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Well, she looked yesternight fairer than ever I saw
  3. her look, or any woman else.
  1. TROILUS
  2. I was about to tell thee: — when my heart,
  3. As wedged with a sigh, would rive in twain,
  4. Lest Hector or my father should perceive me,
  5. I have, as when the sun doth light a storm,
  6. Buried this sigh in wrinkle of a smile:
  7. But sorrow, that is couch'd in seeming gladness,
  8. Is like that mirth fate turns to sudden sadness.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. An her hair were not somewhat darker than Helen's —
  3. well, go to — there were no more comparison between
  4. the women: but, for my part, she is my kinswoman; I
  5. would not, as they term it, praise her: but I would
  6. somebody had heard her talk yesterday, as I did. I
  7. will not dispraise your sister Cassandra's wit, but —
  1. TROILUS
  2. O Pandarus! I tell thee, Pandarus —
  3. When I do tell thee, there my hopes lie drown'd,
  4. Reply not in how many fathoms deep
  5. They lie indrench'd. I tell thee I am mad
  6. In Cressid's love: thou answer'st 'she is fair;'
  7. Pour'st in the open ulcer of my heart
  8. Her eyes, her hair, her cheek, her gait, her voice,
  9. Handlest in thy discourse, O, that her hand,
  10. In whose comparison all whites are ink,
  11. Writing their own reproach, to whose soft seizure
  12. The cygnet's down is harsh and spirit of sense
  13. Hard as the palm of ploughman: this thou tell'st me,
  14. As true thou tell'st me, when I say I love her;
  15. But, saying thus, instead of oil and balm,
  16. Thou lay'st in every gash that love hath given me
  17. The knife that made it.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. I speak no more than truth.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Thou dost not speak so much.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Faith, I'll not meddle in't. Let her be as she is:
  3. if she be fair, 'tis the better for her; an she be
  4. not, she has the mends in her own hands.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Good Pandarus, how now, Pandarus!
  1. PANDARUS
  2. I have had my labour for my travail; ill-thought on of
  3. her and ill-thought on of you; gone between and
  4. between, but small thanks for my labour.
  1. TROILUS
  2. What, art thou angry, Pandarus? what, with me?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Because she's kin to me, therefore she's not so fair
  3. as Helen: an she were not kin to me, she would be as
  4. fair on Friday as Helen is on Sunday. But what care
  5. I? I care not an she were a black-a-moor; 'tis all one to me.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Say I she is not fair?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. I do not care whether you do or no. She's a fool to
  3. stay behind her father; let her to the Greeks; and so
  4. I'll tell her the next time I see her: for my part,
  5. I'll meddle nor make no more i' the matter.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Pandarus —
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Not I.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Sweet Pandarus —
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Pray you, speak no more to me: I will leave all as I
  3. found it, and there an end.
Exit PANDARUS. An alarum
  1. TROILUS
  2. Peace, you ungracious clamours! peace, rude sounds!
  3. Fools on both sides! Helen must needs be fair,
  4. When with your blood you daily paint her thus.
  5. I cannot fight upon this argument;
  6. It is too starved a subject for my sword.
  7. But Pandarus — O gods, how do you plague me!
  8. I cannot come to Cressid but by Pandar;
  9. And he's as tetchy to be woo'd to woo.
  10. As she is stubborn-chaste against all suit.
  11. Tell me, Apollo, for thy Daphne's love,
  12. What Cressid is, what Pandar, and what we?
  13. Her bed is India; there she lies, a pearl:
  14. Between our Ilium and where she resides,
  15. Let it be call'd the wild and wandering flood,
  16. Ourself the merchant, and this sailing Pandar
  17. Our doubtful hope, our convoy and our bark.
Alarum. Enter AENEAS
  1. AENEAS
  2. How now, Prince Troilus! wherefore not afield?
  1. TROILUS
  2. Because not there: this woman's answer sorts,
  3. For womanish it is to be from thence.
  4. What news, AEneas, from the field to-day?
  1. AENEAS
  2. That Paris is returned home and hurt.
  1. TROILUS
  2. By whom, AEneas?
  1. AENEAS
  2. Troilus, by Menelaus.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Let Paris bleed; 'tis but a scar to scorn;
  3. Paris is gored with Menelaus' horn.
Alarum
  1. AENEAS
  2. Hark, what good sport is out of town to-day!
  1. TROILUS
  2. Better at home, if 'would I might' were 'may.'
  3. But to the sport abroad: are you bound thither?
  1. AENEAS
  2. In all swift haste.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Come, go we then together.
Exeunt

SCENE II. The Same. A street.

Enter CRESSIDA and ALEXANDER
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Who were those went by?
  1. ALEXANDER
  2. Queen Hecuba and Helen.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. And whither go they?
  1. ALEXANDER
  2. Up to the eastern tower,
  3. Whose height commands as subject all the vale,
  4. To see the battle. Hector, whose patience
  5. Is, as a virtue, fix'd, to-day was moved:
  6. He chid Andromache and struck his armourer,
  7. And, like as there were husbandry in war,
  8. Before the sun rose he was harness'd light,
  9. And to the field goes he; where every flower
  10. Did, as a prophet, weep what it foresaw
  11. In Hector's wrath.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. What was his cause of anger?
  1. ALEXANDER
  2. The noise goes, this: there is among the Greeks
  3. A lord of Trojan blood, nephew to Hector;
  4. They call him Ajax.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Good; and what of him?
  1. ALEXANDER
  2. They say he is a very man per se,
  3. And stands alone.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. So do all men, unless they are drunk, sick, or have no legs.
  1. ALEXANDER
  2. This man, lady, hath robbed many beasts of their
  3. particular additions; he is as valiant as the lion,
  4. churlish as the bear, slow as the elephant: a man
  5. into whom nature hath so crowded humours that his
  6. valour is crushed into folly, his folly sauced with
  7. discretion: there is no man hath a virtue that he
  8. hath not a glimpse of, nor any man an attaint but he
  9. carries some stain of it: he is melancholy without
  10. cause, and merry against the hair: he hath the
  11. joints of every thing, but everything so out of joint
  12. that he is a gouty Briareus, many hands and no use,
  13. or purblind Argus, all eyes and no sight.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. But how should this man, that makes
  3. me smile, make Hector angry?
  1. ALEXANDER
  2. They say he yesterday coped Hector in the battle and
  3. struck him down, the disdain and shame whereof hath
  4. ever since kept Hector fasting and waking.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Who comes here?
  1. ALEXANDER
  2. Madam, your uncle Pandarus.
Enter PANDARUS
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Hector's a gallant man.
  1. ALEXANDER
  2. As may be in the world, lady.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. What's that? what's that?
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Good morrow, uncle Pandarus.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Good morrow, cousin Cressid: what do you talk of?
  3. Good morrow, Alexander. How do you, cousin? When
  4. were you at Ilium?
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. This morning, uncle.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. What were you talking of when I came? Was Hector
  3. armed and gone ere ye came to Ilium? Helen was not
  4. up, was she?
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Hector was gone, but Helen was not up.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Even so: Hector was stirring early.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. That were we talking of, and of his anger.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Was he angry?
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. So he says here.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. True, he was so: I know the cause too: he'll lay
  3. about him to-day, I can tell them that: and there's
  4. Troilus will not come far behind him: let them take
  5. heed of Troilus, I can tell them that too.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. What, is he angry too?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Who, Troilus? Troilus is the better man of the two.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. O Jupiter! there's no comparison.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. What, not between Troilus and Hector? Do you know a
  3. man if you see him?
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Ay, if I ever saw him before and knew him.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Well, I say Troilus is Troilus.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Then you say as I say; for, I am sure, he is not Hector.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. No, nor Hector is not Troilus in some degrees.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. 'Tis just to each of them; he is himself.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Himself! Alas, poor Troilus! I would he were.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. So he is.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Condition, I had gone barefoot to India.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. He is not Hector.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Himself! no, he's not himself: would a' were
  3. himself! Well, the gods are above; time must friend
  4. or end: well, Troilus, well: I would my heart were
  5. in her body. No, Hector is not a better man than Troilus.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Excuse me.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. He is elder.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Pardon me, pardon me.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Th' other's not come to't; you shall tell me another
  3. tale, when th' other's come to't. Hector shall not
  4. have his wit this year.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. He shall not need it, if he have his own.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Nor his qualities.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. No matter.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Nor his beauty.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. 'Twould not become him; his own's better.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. You have no judgment, niece: Helen
  3. herself swore th' other day, that Troilus, for
  4. a brown favour — for so 'tis, I must confess —
  5. not brown neither —
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. No, but brown.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. 'Faith, to say truth, brown and not brown.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. To say the truth, true and not true.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. She praised his complexion above Paris.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Why, Paris hath colour enough.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. So he has.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Then Troilus should have too much: if she praised
  3. him above, his complexion is higher than his; he
  4. having colour enough, and the other higher, is too
  5. flaming a praise for a good complexion. I had as
  6. lief Helen's golden tongue had commended Troilus for
  7. a copper nose.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. I swear to you. I think Helen loves him better than Paris.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Then she's a merry Greek indeed.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Nay, I am sure she does. She came to him th' other
  3. day into the compassed window — and, you know, he
  4. has not past three or four hairs on his chin —
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Indeed, a tapster's arithmetic may soon bring his
  3. particulars therein to a total.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Why, he is very young: and yet will he, within
  3. three pound, lift as much as his brother Hector.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Is he so young a man and so old a lifter?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. But to prove to you that Helen loves him: she came
  3. and puts me her white hand to his cloven chin —
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Juno have mercy! how came it cloven?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Why, you know 'tis dimpled: I think his smiling
  3. becomes him better than any man in all Phrygia.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. O, he smiles valiantly.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Does he not?
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. O yes, an 'twere a cloud in autumn.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Why, go to, then: but to prove to you that Helen
  3. loves Troilus —
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Troilus will stand to the proof, if you'll
  3. prove it so.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Troilus! why, he esteems her no more than I esteem
  3. an addle egg.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. If you love an addle egg as well as you love an idle
  3. head, you would eat chickens i' the shell.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. I cannot choose but laugh, to think how she tickled
  3. his chin: indeed, she has a marvellous white hand, I
  4. must needs confess —
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Without the rack.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. And she takes upon her to spy a white hair on his chin.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Alas, poor chin! many a wart is richer.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. But there was such laughing! Queen Hecuba laughed
  3. that her eyes ran o'er.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. With mill-stones.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. And Cassandra laughed.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. But there was more temperate fire under the pot of
  3. her eyes: did her eyes run o'er too?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. And Hector laughed.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. At what was all this laughing?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Marry, at the white hair that Helen spied on Troilus' chin.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. An't had been a green hair, I should have laughed
  3. too.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. They laughed not so much at the hair as at his pretty answer.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. What was his answer?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Quoth she, 'Here's but two and fifty hairs on your
  3. chin, and one of them is white.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. This is her question.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. That's true; make no question of that. 'Two and
  3. fifty hairs' quoth he, 'and one white: that white
  4. hair is my father, and all the rest are his sons.'
  5. 'Jupiter!' quoth she, 'which of these hairs is Paris,
  6. my husband? 'The forked one,' quoth he, 'pluck't
  7. out, and give it him.' But there was such laughing!
  8. and Helen so blushed, an Paris so chafed, and all the
  9. rest so laughed, that it passed.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. So let it now; for it has been while going by.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Well, cousin. I told you a thing yesterday; think on't.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. So I do.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. I'll be sworn 'tis true; he will weep you, an 'twere
  3. a man born in April.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. And I'll spring up in his tears, an 'twere a nettle
  3. against May.
A retreat sounded
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Hark! they are coming from the field: shall we
  3. stand up here, and see them as they pass toward
  4. Ilium? good niece, do, sweet niece Cressida.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. At your pleasure.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Here, here, here's an excellent place; here we may
  3. see most bravely: I'll tell you them all by their
  4. names as they pass by; but mark Troilus above the rest.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Speak not so loud.
AENEAS passes
  1. PANDARUS
  2. That's AEneas: is not that a brave man? he's one of
  3. the flowers of Troy, I can tell you: but mark
  4. Troilus; you shall see anon.
ANTENOR passes
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Who's that?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. That's Antenor: he has a shrewd wit, I can tell you;
  3. and he's a man good enough, he's one o' the soundest
  4. judgments in whosoever, and a proper man of person.
  5. When comes Troilus? I'll show you Troilus anon: if
  6. he see me, you shall see him nod at me.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Will he give you the nod?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. You shall see.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. If he do, the rich shall have more.
HECTOR passes
  1. PANDARUS
  2. That's Hector, that, that, look you, that; there's a
  3. fellow! Go thy way, Hector! There's a brave man,
  4. niece. O brave Hector! Look how he looks! there's
  5. a countenance! is't not a brave man?
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. O, a brave man!
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Is a' not? it does a man's heart good. Look you
  3. what hacks are on his helmet! look you yonder, do
  4. you see? look you there: there's no jesting;
  5. there's laying on, take't off who will, as they say:
  6. there be hacks!
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Be those with swords?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Swords! any thing, he cares not; an the devil come
  3. to him, it's all one: by God's lid, it does one's
  4. heart good. Yonder comes Paris, yonder comes Paris.
  5. PARIS passes
  6. Look ye yonder, niece; is't not a gallant man too,
  7. is't not? Why, this is brave now. Who said he came
  8. hurt home to-day? he's not hurt: why, this will do
  9. Helen's heart good now, ha! Would I could see
  10. Troilus now! You shall see Troilus anon.
HELENUS passes
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Who's that?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. That's Helenus. I marvel where Troilus is. That's
  3. Helenus. I think he went not forth to-day. That's Helenus.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Can Helenus fight, uncle?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Helenus? no. Yes, he'll fight indifferent well. I
  3. marvel where Troilus is. Hark! do you not hear the
  4. people cry 'Troilus'? Helenus is a priest.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. What sneaking fellow comes yonder?
TROILUS passes
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Where? yonder? that's Deiphobus. 'Tis Troilus!
  3. there's a man, niece! Hem! Brave Troilus! the
  4. prince of chivalry!
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Peace, for shame, peace!
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Mark him; note him. O brave Troilus! Look well upon

  9. Paris is dirt to him; and, I warrant, Helen, to

  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Here come more.
Forces pass
  1. PANDARUS

  2. There is among the Greeks Achilles, a better man than Troilus.

  2. Achilles! a drayman, a porter, a very camel.

  2. Ay, a minced man: and then to be baked with no date

  2. You are such a woman! one knows not at what ward you

  1. CRESSIDA

  1. PANDARUS
  2. Say one of your watches.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. Nay, I'll watch you for that; and that's one of the

  4. not have hit, I can watch you for telling how I took
  5. the blow; unless it swell past hiding, and then it's
  6. past watching.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. You are such another!

  1. PANDARUS
  2. Good boy, tell him I come.

  4. I doubt he be hurt. Fare ye well, good niece.
  1. CRESSIDA

  2. I'll be with you, niece, by and by.
  1. CRESSIDA
  2. To bring, uncle?
  1. PANDARUS

  6. If there be rule in unity itself,
  7. This is not she. O madness of discourse,
  8. That cause sets up with and against itself!
  9. Bi-fold authority! where reason can revolt
  10. Without perdition, and loss assume all reason
  11. Without revolt: this is, and is not, Cressid.
  12. Within my soul there doth conduce a fight
  13. Of this strange nature that a thing inseparate
  14. Divides more wider than the sky and earth,
  15. And yet the spacious breadth of this division
  16. Admits no orifex for a point as subtle
  17. As Ariachne's broken woof to enter.
  18. Instance, O instance! strong as Pluto's gates;
  19. Cressid is mine, tied with the bonds of heaven:
  20. Instance, O instance! strong as heaven itself;
  21. The bonds of heaven are slipp'd, dissolved, and loosed;
  22. And with another knot, five-finger-tied,
  23. The fractions of her faith, orts of her love,
  24. The fragments, scraps, the bits and greasy relics
  25. Of her o'er-eaten faith, are bound to Diomed.
  1. ULYSSES
  2. May worthy Troilus be half attach'd
  3. With that which here his passion doth express?
  1. TROILUS
  2. Ay, Greek; and that shall be divulged well
  3. In characters as red as Mars his heart
  4. Inflamed with Venus: never did young man fancy
  5. With so eternal and so fix'd a soul.
  6. Hark, Greek: as much as I do Cressid love,
  7. So much by weight hate I her Diomed:
  8. That sleeve is mine that he'll bear on his helm;
  9. Were it a casque composed by Vulcan's skill,
  10. My sword should bite it: not the dreadful spout
  11. Which shipmen do the hurricano call,
  12. Constringed in mass by the almighty sun,
  13. Shall dizzy with more clamour Neptune's ear
  14. In his descent than shall my prompted sword
  15. Falling on Diomed.
  1. THERSITES
  2. He'll tickle it for his concupy.
  1. TROILUS
  2. O Cressid! O false Cressid! false, false, false!
  3. Let all untruths stand by thy stained name,
  4. And they'll seem glorious.
  1. ULYSSES
  2. O, contain yourself
  3. Your passion draws ears hither.
Enter AENEAS
  1. AENEAS
  2. I have been seeking you this hour, my lord:
  3. Hector, by this, is arming him in Troy;
  4. Ajax, your guard, stays to conduct you home.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Have with you, prince. My courteous lord, adieu.
  3. Farewell, revolted fair! and, Diomed,
  4. Stand fast, and wear a castle on thy head!
  1. ULYSSES
  2. I'll bring you to the gates.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Accept distracted thanks.
Exeunt TROILUS, AENEAS, and ULYSSES
  1. THERSITES
  2. Would I could meet that rogue Diomed! I would
  3. croak like a raven; I would bode, I would bode.
  4. Patroclus will give me any thing for the
  5. intelligence of this whore: the parrot will not
  6. do more for an almond than he for a commodious drab.
  7. Lechery, lechery; still, wars and lechery; nothing
  8. else holds fashion: a burning devil take them!
Exit

Enter HECTOR and ANDROMACHE
  1. ANDROMACHE
  2. When was my lord so much ungently temper'd,
  3. To stop his ears against admonishment?
  4. Unarm, unarm, and do not fight to-day.
  1. HECTOR
  2. You train me to offend you; get you in:
  3. By all the everlasting gods, I'll go!
  1. ANDROMACHE
  2. My dreams will, sure, prove ominous to the day.
  1. HECTOR
  2. No more, I say.
Enter CASSANDRA
  1. CASSANDRA
  2. Where is my brother Hector?
  1. ANDROMACHE
  2. Here, sister; arm'd, and bloody in intent.
  3. Consort with me in loud and dear petition,
  4. Pursue we him on knees; for I have dream'd
  5. Of bloody turbulence, and this whole night
  6. Hath nothing been but shapes and forms of slaughter.
  1. CASSANDRA
  2. O, 'tis true.
  1. HECTOR
  2. Ho! bid my trumpet sound!
  1. CASSANDRA
  2. No notes of sally, for the heavens, sweet brother.
  1. HECTOR
  2. Be gone, I say: the gods have heard me swear.
  1. CASSANDRA
  2. The gods are deaf to hot and peevish vows:
  3. They are polluted offerings, more abhorr'd
  4. Than spotted livers in the sacrifice.
  1. ANDROMACHE
  2. O, be persuaded! do not count it holy
  3. To hurt by being just: it is as lawful,
  4. For we would give much, to use violent thefts,
  5. And rob in the behalf of charity.
  1. CASSANDRA
  2. It is the purpose that makes strong the vow;
  3. But vows to every purpose must not hold:
  4. Unarm, sweet Hector.
  1. HECTOR
  2. Hold you still, I say;
  3. Mine honour keeps the weather of my fate:
  4. Lie every man holds dear; but the brave man
  5. Holds honour far more precious-dear than life.
  6. Enter TROILUS
  7. How now, young man! mean'st thou to fight to-day?
  1. ANDROMACHE
  2. Cassandra, call my father to persuade.
Exit CASSANDRA
  1. HECTOR
  2. No, faith, young Troilus; doff thy harness, youth;
  3. I am to-day i' the vein of chivalry:
  4. Let grow thy sinews till their knots be strong,
  5. And tempt not yet the brushes of the war.
  6. Unarm thee, go, and doubt thou not, brave boy,
  7. I'll stand to-day for thee and me and Troy.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Brother, you have a vice of mercy in you,
  3. Which better fits a lion than a man.
  1. HECTOR
  2. What vice is that, good Troilus? chide me for it.
  1. TROILUS
  2. When many times the captive Grecian falls,
  3. Even in the fan and wind of your fair sword,
  4. You bid them rise, and live.
  1. HECTOR
  2. O,'tis fair play.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Fool's play, by heaven, Hector.
  1. HECTOR
  2. How now! how now!
  1. TROILUS
  2. For the love of all the gods,
  3. Let's leave the hermit pity with our mothers,
  4. And when we have our armours buckled on,
  5. The venom'd vengeance ride upon our swords,
  6. Spur them to ruthful work, rein them from ruth.
  1. HECTOR
  2. Fie, savage, fie!
  1. TROILUS
  2. Hector, then 'tis wars.
  1. HECTOR
  2. Troilus, I would not have you fight to-day.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Who should withhold me?
  3. Not fate, obedience, nor the hand of Mars
  4. Beckoning with fiery truncheon my retire;
  5. Not Priamus and Hecuba on knees,
  6. Their eyes o'ergalled with recourse of tears;
  7. Not you, my brother, with your true sword drawn,
  8. Opposed to hinder me, should stop my way,
  9. But by my ruin.
Re-enter CASSANDRA, with PRIAM
  1. CASSANDRA
  2. Lay hold upon him, Priam, hold him fast:
  3. He is thy crutch; now if thou lose thy stay,
  4. Thou on him leaning, and all Troy on thee,
  5. Fall all together.
  1. PRIAM
  2. Come, Hector, come, go back:
  3. Thy wife hath dream'd; thy mother hath had visions;
  4. Cassandra doth foresee; and I myself
  5. Am like a prophet suddenly enrapt
  6. To tell thee that this day is ominous:
  7. Therefore, come back.
  1. HECTOR
  2. AEneas is a-field;
  3. And I do stand engaged to many Greeks,
  4. Even in the faith of valour, to appear
  5. This morning to them.
  1. PRIAM
  2. Ay, but thou shalt not go.
  1. HECTOR
  2. I must not break my faith.
  3. You know me dutiful; therefore, dear sir,
  4. Let me not shame respect; but give me leave
  5. To take that course by your consent and voice,
  6. Which you do here forbid me, royal Priam.
  1. CASSANDRA
  2. O Priam, yield not to him!
  1. ANDROMACHE
  2. Do not, dear father.
  1. HECTOR
  2. Andromache, I am offended with you:
  3. Upon the love you bear me, get you in.
Exit ANDROMACHE
  1. TROILUS
  2. This foolish, dreaming, superstitious girl
  3. Makes all these bodements.
  1. CASSANDRA
  2. O, farewell, dear Hector!
  3. Look, how thou diest! look, how thy eye turns pale!
  4. Look, how thy wounds do bleed at many vents!
  5. Hark, how Troy roars! how Hecuba cries out!
  6. How poor Andromache shrills her dolours forth!
  7. Behold, distraction, frenzy and amazement,
  8. Like witless antics, one another meet,
  9. And all cry, Hector! Hector's dead! O Hector!
  1. TROILUS
  2. Away! away!
  1. CASSANDRA
  2. Farewell: yet, soft! Hector! take my leave:
  3. Thou dost thyself and all our Troy deceive.
Exit
  1. HECTOR
  2. You are amazed, my liege, at her exclaim:
  3. Go in and cheer the town: we'll forth and fight,
  4. Do deeds worth praise and tell you them at night.
  1. PRIAM
  2. Farewell: the gods with safety stand about thee!
Exeunt severally PRIAM and HECTOR. Alarums
  1. TROILUS
  2. They are at it, hark! Proud Diomed, believe,
  3. I come to lose my arm, or win my sleeve.
Enter PANDARUS
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Do you hear, my lord? do you hear?
  1. TROILUS
  2. What now?
  1. PANDARUS
  2. Here's a letter come from yond poor girl.
  1. TROILUS
  2. Let me read.
  1. PANDARUS
  2. A whoreson tisick, a whoreson rascally tisick so
  3. troubles me, and the foolish fortune of this girl;
  4. and what one thing, what another, that I shall
  5. leave you one o' these days: and I have a rheum
  6. in mine eyes too, and such an ache in my bones
  7. that, unless a man were cursed, I cannot tell what
  8. to think on't. What says she there?
  1. TROILUS
  2. Words, words, mere words, no matter from the heart:
  3. The effect doth operate another way.
  4. Tearing the letter
  5. Go, wind, to wind, there turn and change together.
  6. My love with words and errors still she feeds;
  7. But edifies another with her deeds.
Exeunt severally

SCENE IV. Plains between Troy and the Grecian camp.

Alarums: excursions. Enter THERSITES
  1. THERSITES
  2. Now they are clapper-clawing one another; I'll go
  3. look on. That dissembling abominable varlets Diomed,
  4. has got that same scurvy doting foolish young knave's
  5. sleeve of Troy there in his helm: I would fain see
  6. them meet; that that same young Trojan ass, that
  7. loves the whore there, might send that Greekish
  8. whore-masterly villain, with the sleeve, back to the
  9. dissembling luxurious drab, of a sleeveless errand.
  10. O' the t'other side, the policy of those crafty
  11. swearing rascals, that stale old mouse-eaten dry
  12. cheese, Nestor, and that same dog-fox, Ulysses, is
  13. not proved worthy a blackberry: they set me up, in
  14. policy, that mongrel cur, Ajax, against that dog of
  15. as bad a kind, Achilles: and now is the cur Ajax
  16. prouder than the cur Achilles, and will not arm
  17. to-day; whereupon the Grecians begin to proclaim
  18. barbarism, and policy grows into an ill opinion.
  19. Soft! here comes sleeve, and t'other.
Enter DIOMEDES, TROILUS following
  1. TROILUS
  2. Fly not; for shouldst thou take the river Styx,
  3. I would swim after.
  1. DIOMEDES
  2. Thou dost miscall retire:
  3. I do not fly, but advantageous care
  4. Withdrew me from the odds of multitude:
  5. Have at thee!
  1. THERSITES
  2. Hold thy whore, Grecian! — now for thy whore,
  3. Trojan! — now the sleeve, now the sleeve!
Exeunt TROILUS and DIOMEDES, fighting

  2. What art thou, Greek? art thou for Hector's match?

  1. HECTOR
  2. I do believe thee: live.
Exit

  6. miracle: yet, in a sort, lechery eats itself.

SCENE X. Another part of the plains.

Enter AENEAS and Trojans
  1. AENEAS
  2. Stand, ho! yet are we masters of the field:
  3. Never go home; here starve we out the night.
Enter TROILUS
  1. TROILUS
  2. Hector is slain.
  1. ALL
  2. Hector! the gods forbid!
  1. TROILUS
  2. He's dead; and at the murderer's horse's tail,
  3. In beastly sort, dragg'd through the shameful field.
  4. Frown on, you heavens, effect your rage with speed!
  5. Sit, gods, upon your thrones, and smile at Troy!
  6. I say, at once let your brief plagues be mercy,
  7. And linger not our sure destructions on!
  1. AENEAS
  2. My lord, you do discomfort all the host!
  1. TROILUS
  2. You understand me not that tell me so:
  3. I do not speak of flight, of fear, of death,
  4. But dare all imminence that gods and men
  5. Address their dangers in. Hector is gone:
  6. Who shall tell Priam so, or Hecuba?
  7. Let him that will a screech-owl aye be call'd,
  8. Go in to Troy, and say there, Hector's dead:
  9. There is a word will Priam turn to stone;
  10. Make wells and Niobes of the maids and wives,
  11. Cold statues of the youth, and, in a word,
  12. Scare Troy out of itself. But, march away:
  13. Hector is dead; there is no more to say.
  14. Stay yet. You vile abominable tents,
  15. Thus proudly pight upon our Phrygian plains,
  16. Let Titan rise as early as he dare,
  17. I'll through and through you! and, thou great-sized coward,
  18. No space of earth shall sunder our two hates:
  19. I'll haunt thee like a wicked conscience still,
  20. That mouldeth goblins swift as frenzy's thoughts.
  21. Strike a free march to Troy! with comfort go:
  22. Hope of revenge shall hide our inward woe.
Exeunt AENEAS and Trojans

  1. PANDARUS
  2. But hear you, hear you!
  1. TROILUS
  2. Hence, broker-lackey! ignomy and shame
  3. Pursue thy life, and live aye with thy name!

  2. A goodly medicine for my aching bones! O world!
  3. world! world! thus is the poor agent despised!

  6. endeavour be so loved and the performance so loathed?

  8. Full merrily the humble-bee doth sing,
  9. Till he hath lost his honey and his sting;
  10. And being once subdued in armed tail,
  11. Sweet honey and sweet notes together fail.
  12. Good traders in the flesh, set this in your
  13. painted cloths.

  18. Brethren and sisters of the hold-door trade,
  19. Some two months hence my will shall here be made:
  20. It should be now, but that my fear is this,
  21. Some galled goose of Winchester would hiss:
  22. Till then I'll sweat and seek about for eases,
  23. And at that time bequeathe you my diseases.
