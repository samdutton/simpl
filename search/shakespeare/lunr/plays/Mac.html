SCENE Scotland: England.

ACT I

SCENE I. A desert place.

Thunder and lightning. Enter three Witches
  1. First Witch
  2. When shall we three meet again
  3. In thunder, lightning, or in rain?
  1. Second Witch
  2. When the hurlyburly's done,
  3. When the battle's lost and won.
  1. Third Witch
  2. That will be ere the set of sun.
  1. First Witch
  2. Where the place?
  1. Second Witch
  2. Upon the heath.
  1. Third Witch
  2. There to meet with Macbeth.
  1. First Witch
  2. I come, Graymalkin!
  1. Second Witch
  2. Paddock calls.
  1. Third Witch
  2. Anon.
  1. ALL
  2. Fair is foul, and foul is fair:
  3. Hover through the fog and filthy air.
Exeunt

SCENE II. A camp near Forres.

Alarum within. Enter DUNCAN, MALCOLM, DONALBAIN, LENNOX, with Attendants, meeting a bleeding Sergeant
  1. DUNCAN
  2. What bloody man is that? He can report,
  3. As seemeth by his plight, of the revolt
  4. The newest state.
  1. MALCOLM
  2. This is the sergeant
  3. Who like a good and hardy soldier fought
  4. 'Gainst my captivity. Hail, brave friend!
  5. Say to the king the knowledge of the broil
  6. As thou didst leave it.
  1. Sergeant
  2. Doubtful it stood;
  3. As two spent swimmers, that do cling together
  4. And choke their art. The merciless Macdonwald —
  5. Worthy to be a rebel, for to that
  6. The multiplying villanies of nature
  7. Do swarm upon him — from the western isles
  8. Of kerns and gallowglasses is supplied;
  9. And fortune, on his damned quarrel smiling,
  10. Show'd like a rebel's whore: but all's too weak:
  11. For brave Macbeth — well he deserves that name —
  12. Disdaining fortune, with his brandish'd steel,
  13. Which smoked with bloody execution,
  14. Like valour's minion carved out his passage
  15. Till he faced the slave;
  16. Which ne'er shook hands, nor bade farewell to him,
  17. Till he unseam'd him from the nave to the chaps,
  18. And fix'd his head upon our battlements.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. O valiant cousin! worthy gentleman!
  1. Sergeant
  2. As whence the sun 'gins his reflection
  3. Shipwrecking storms and direful thunders break,
  4. So from that spring whence comfort seem'd to come
  5. Discomfort swells. Mark, king of Scotland, mark:
  6. No sooner justice had with valour arm'd
  7. Compell'd these skipping kerns to trust their heels,
  8. But the Norweyan lord surveying vantage,
  9. With furbish'd arms and new supplies of men
  10. Began a fresh assault.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. Dismay'd not this
  3. Our captains, Macbeth and Banquo?
  1. Sergeant
  2. Yes;
  3. As sparrows eagles, or the hare the lion.
  4. If I say sooth, I must report they were
  5. As cannons overcharged with double cracks, so they
  6. Doubly redoubled strokes upon the foe:
  7. Except they meant to bathe in reeking wounds,
  8. Or memorise another Golgotha,
  9. I cannot tell.
  10. But I am faint, my gashes cry for help.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. So well thy words become thee as thy wounds;
  3. They smack of honour both. Go get him surgeons.
  4. Exit Sergeant, attended
  5. Who comes here?
Enter ROSS
  1. MALCOLM
  2. The worthy thane of Ross.
  1. LENNOX
  2. What a haste looks through his eyes! So should he look
  3. That seems to speak things strange.
  1. ROSS
  2. God save the king!
  1. DUNCAN
  2. Whence camest thou, worthy thane?
  1. ROSS
  2. From Fife, great king;
  3. Where the Norweyan banners flout the sky
  4. And fan our people cold. Norway himself,
  5. With terrible numbers,
  6. Assisted by that most disloyal traitor
  7. The thane of Cawdor, began a dismal conflict;
  8. Till that Bellona's bridegroom, lapp'd in proof,
  9. Confronted him with self-comparisons,
  10. Point against point rebellious, arm 'gainst arm.
  11. Curbing his lavish spirit: and, to conclude,
  12. The victory fell on us.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. Great happiness!
  1. ROSS
  2. That now
  3. Sweno, the Norways' king, craves composition:
  4. Nor would we deign him burial of his men
  5. Till he disbursed at Saint Colme's inch
  6. Ten thousand dollars to our general use.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. No more that thane of Cawdor shall deceive
  3. Our bosom interest: go pronounce his present death,
  4. And with his former title greet Macbeth.
  1. ROSS
  2. I'll see it done.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. What he hath lost noble Macbeth hath won.
Exeunt

SCENE III. A heath near Forres.

Thunder. Enter the three Witches
  1. First Witch
  2. Where hast thou been, sister?
  1. Second Witch
  2. Killing swine.
  1. Third Witch
  2. Sister, where thou?
  1. First Witch
  2. A sailor's wife had chestnuts in her lap,
  3. And munch'd, and munch'd, and munch'd: —
  4. 'Give me,' quoth I:
  5. 'Aroint thee, witch!' the rump-fed ronyon cries.
  6. Her husband's to Aleppo gone, master o' the Tiger:
  7. But in a sieve I'll thither sail,
  8. And, like a rat without a tail,
  9. I'll do, I'll do, and I'll do.
  1. Second Witch
  2. I'll give thee a wind.
  1. First Witch
  2. Thou'rt kind.
  1. Third Witch
  2. And I another.
  1. First Witch
  2. I myself have all the other,
  3. And the very ports they blow,
  4. All the quarters that they know
  5. I' the shipman's card.
  6. I will drain him dry as hay:
  7. Sleep shall neither night nor day
  8. Hang upon his pent-house lid;
  9. He shall live a man forbid:
  10. Weary se'nnights nine times nine
  11. Shall he dwindle, peak and pine:
  12. Though his bark cannot be lost,
  13. Yet it shall be tempest-tost.
  14. Look what I have.
  1. Second Witch
  2. Show me, show me.
  1. First Witch
  2. Here I have a pilot's thumb,
  3. Wreck'd as homeward he did come.
Drum within
  1. Third Witch
  2. A drum, a drum!
  3. Macbeth doth come.
  1. ALL
  2. The weird sisters, hand in hand,
  3. Posters of the sea and land,
  4. Thus do go about, about:
  5. Thrice to thine and thrice to mine
  6. And thrice again, to make up nine.
  7. Peace! the charm's wound up.
Enter MACBETH and BANQUO
  1. MACBETH
  2. So foul and fair a day I have not seen.
  1. BANQUO
  2. How far is't call'd to Forres? What are these
  3. So wither'd and so wild in their attire,
  4. That look not like the inhabitants o' the earth,
  5. And yet are on't? Live you? or are you aught
  6. That man may question? You seem to understand me,
  7. By each at once her chappy finger laying
  8. Upon her skinny lips: you should be women,
  9. And yet your beards forbid me to interpret
  10. That you are so.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Speak, if you can: what are you?
  1. First Witch
  2. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, thane of Glamis!
  1. Second Witch
  2. All hail, Macbeth, hail to thee, thane of Cawdor!
  1. Third Witch
  2. All hail, Macbeth, thou shalt be king hereafter!
  1. BANQUO
  2. Good sir, why do you start; and seem to fear
  3. Things that do sound so fair? I' the name of truth,
  4. Are ye fantastical, or that indeed
  5. Which outwardly ye show? My noble partner
  6. You greet with present grace and great prediction
  7. Of noble having and of royal hope,
  8. That he seems rapt withal: to me you speak not.
  9. If you can look into the seeds of time,
  10. And say which grain will grow and which will not,
  11. Speak then to me, who neither beg nor fear
  12. Your favours nor your hate.
  1. First Witch
  2. Hail!
  1. Second Witch
  2. Hail!
  1. Third Witch
  2. Hail!
  1. First Witch
  2. Lesser than Macbeth, and greater.
  1. Second Witch
  2. Not so happy, yet much happier.
  1. Third Witch
  2. Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none:
  3. So all hail, Macbeth and Banquo!
  1. First Witch
  2. Banquo and Macbeth, all hail!
  1. MACBETH
  2. Stay, you imperfect speakers, tell me more:
  3. By Sinel's death I know I am thane of Glamis;
  4. But how of Cawdor? the thane of Cawdor lives,
  5. A prosperous gentleman; and to be king
  6. Stands not within the prospect of belief,
  7. No more than to be Cawdor. Say from whence
  8. You owe this strange intelligence? or why
  9. Upon this blasted heath you stop our way
  10. With such prophetic greeting? Speak, I charge you.
Witches vanish
  1. BANQUO
  2. The earth hath bubbles, as the water has,
  3. And these are of them. Whither are they vanish'd?
  1. MACBETH
  2. Into the air; and what seem'd corporal melted
  3. As breath into the wind. Would they had stay'd!
  1. BANQUO
  2. Were such things here as we do speak about?
  3. Or have we eaten on the insane root
  4. That takes the reason prisoner?
  1. MACBETH
  2. Your children shall be kings.
  1. BANQUO
  2. You shall be king.
  1. MACBETH
  2. And thane of Cawdor too: went it not so?
  1. BANQUO
  2. To the selfsame tune and words. Who's here?
Enter ROSS and ANGUS
  1. ROSS
  2. The king hath happily received, Macbeth,
  3. The news of thy success; and when he reads
  4. Thy personal venture in the rebels' fight,
  5. His wonders and his praises do contend
  6. Which should be thine or his: silenced with that,
  7. In viewing o'er the rest o' the selfsame day,
  8. He finds thee in the stout Norweyan ranks,
  9. Nothing afeard of what thyself didst make,
  10. Strange images of death. As thick as hail
  11. Came post with post; and every one did bear
  12. Thy praises in his kingdom's great defence,
  13. And pour'd them down before him.
  1. ANGUS
  2. We are sent
  3. To give thee from our royal master thanks;
  4. Only to herald thee into his sight,
  5. Not pay thee.
  1. ROSS
  2. And, for an earnest of a greater honour,
  3. He bade me, from him, call thee thane of Cawdor:
  4. In which addition, hail, most worthy thane!
  5. For it is thine.
  1. BANQUO
  2. What, can the devil speak true?
  1. MACBETH
  2. The thane of Cawdor lives: why do you dress me
  3. In borrow'd robes?
  1. ANGUS
  2. Who was the thane lives yet;
  3. But under heavy judgment bears that life
  4. Which he deserves to lose. Whether he was combined
  5. With those of Norway, or did line the rebel
  6. With hidden help and vantage, or that with both
  7. He labour'd in his country's wreck, I know not;
  8. But treasons capital, confess'd and proved,
  9. Have overthrown him.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Aside Glamis, and thane of Cawdor!
  3. The greatest is behind.
  4. To ROSS and ANGUS
  5. Thanks for your pains.
  6. To BANQUO
  7. Do you not hope your children shall be kings,
  8. When those that gave the thane of Cawdor to me
  9. Promised no less to them?
  1. BANQUO
  2. That trusted home
  3. Might yet enkindle you unto the crown,
  4. Besides the thane of Cawdor. But 'tis strange:
  5. And oftentimes, to win us to our harm,
  6. The instruments of darkness tell us truths,
  7. Win us with honest trifles, to betray's
  8. In deepest consequence.
  9. Cousins, a word, I pray you.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Aside Two truths are told,
  3. As happy prologues to the swelling act
  4. Of the imperial theme. — I thank you, gentlemen.
  5. Aside This supernatural soliciting
  6. Cannot be ill, cannot be good: if ill,
  7. Why hath it given me earnest of success,
  8. Commencing in a truth? I am thane of Cawdor:
  9. If good, why do I yield to that suggestion
  10. Whose horrid image doth unfix my hair
  11. And make my seated heart knock at my ribs,
  12. Against the use of nature? Present fears
  13. Are less than horrible imaginings:
  14. My thought, whose murder yet is but fantastical,
  15. Shakes so my single state of man that function
  16. Is smother'd in surmise, and nothing is
  17. But what is not.
  1. BANQUO
  2. Look, how our partner's rapt.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Aside If chance will have me king, why, chance may crown me,
  3. Without my stir.
  1. BANQUO
  2. New horrors come upon him,
  3. Like our strange garments, cleave not to their mould
  4. But with the aid of use.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Aside Come what come may,
  3. Time and the hour runs through the roughest day.
  1. BANQUO
  2. Worthy Macbeth, we stay upon your leisure.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Give me your favour: my dull brain was wrought
  3. With things forgotten. Kind gentlemen, your pains
  4. Are register'd where every day I turn
  5. The leaf to read them. Let us toward the king.
  6. Think upon what hath chanced, and, at more time,
  7. The interim having weigh'd it, let us speak
  8. Our free hearts each to other.
  1. BANQUO
  2. Very gladly.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Till then, enough. Come, friends.
Exeunt

SCENE IV. Forres. The palace.

Flourish. Enter DUNCAN, MALCOLM, DONALBAIN, LENNOX, and Attendants
  1. DUNCAN
  2. Is execution done on Cawdor? Are not
  3. Those in commission yet return'd?
  1. MALCOLM
  2. My liege,
  3. They are not yet come back. But I have spoke
  4. With one that saw him die: who did report
  5. That very frankly he confess'd his treasons,
  6. Implored your highness' pardon and set forth
  7. A deep repentance: nothing in his life
  8. Became him like the leaving it; he died
  9. As one that had been studied in his death
  10. To throw away the dearest thing he owed,
  11. As 'twere a careless trifle.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. There's no art
  3. To find the mind's construction in the face:
  4. He was a gentleman on whom I built
  5. An absolute trust.
  6. Enter MACBETH, BANQUO, ROSS, and ANGUS
  7. O worthiest cousin!
  8. The sin of my ingratitude even now
  9. Was heavy on me: thou art so far before
  10. That swiftest wing of recompense is slow
  11. To overtake thee. Would thou hadst less deserved,
  12. That the proportion both of thanks and payment
  13. Might have been mine! only I have left to say,
  14. More is thy due than more than all can pay.
  1. MACBETH
  2. The service and the loyalty I owe,
  3. In doing it, pays itself. Your highness' part
  4. Is to receive our duties; and our duties
  5. Are to your throne and state children and servants,
  6. Which do but what they should, by doing every thing
  7. Safe toward your love and honour.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. Welcome hither:
  3. I have begun to plant thee, and will labour
  4. To make thee full of growing. Noble Banquo,
  5. That hast no less deserved, nor must be known
  6. No less to have done so, let me enfold thee
  7. And hold thee to my heart.
  1. BANQUO
  2. There if I grow,
  3. The harvest is your own.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. My plenteous joys,
  3. Wanton in fulness, seek to hide themselves
  4. In drops of sorrow. Sons, kinsmen, thanes,
  5. And you whose places are the nearest, know
  6. We will establish our estate upon
  7. Our eldest, Malcolm, whom we name hereafter
  8. The Prince of Cumberland; which honour must
  9. Not unaccompanied invest him only,
  10. But signs of nobleness, like stars, shall shine
  11. On all deservers. From hence to Inverness,
  12. And bind us further to you.
  1. MACBETH
  2. The rest is labour, which is not used for you:
  3. I'll be myself the harbinger and make joyful
  4. The hearing of my wife with your approach;
  5. So humbly take my leave.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. My worthy Cawdor!
  1. MACBETH
  2. Aside The Prince of Cumberland! that is a step
  3. On which I must fall down, or else o'erleap,
  4. For in my way it lies. Stars, hide your fires;
  5. Let not light see my black and deep desires:
  6. The eye wink at the hand; yet let that be,
  7. Which the eye fears, when it is done, to see.
Exit
  1. DUNCAN
  2. True, worthy Banquo; he is full so valiant,
  3. And in his commendations I am fed;
  4. It is a banquet to me. Let's after him,
  5. Whose care is gone before to bid us welcome:
  6. It is a peerless kinsman.
Flourish. Exeunt

SCENE V. Inverness. Macbeth's castle.

Enter LADY MACBETH, reading a letter
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. 'They met me in the day of success: and I have
  3. learned by the perfectest report, they have more in
  4. them than mortal knowledge. When I burned in desire
  5. to question them further, they made themselves air,
  6. into which they vanished. Whiles I stood rapt in
  7. the wonder of it, came missives from the king, who
  8. all-hailed me 'Thane of Cawdor;' by which title,
  9. before, these weird sisters saluted me, and referred
  10. me to the coming on of time, with 'Hail, king that
  11. shalt be!' This have I thought good to deliver
  12. thee, my dearest partner of greatness, that thou
  13. mightst not lose the dues of rejoicing, by being
  14. ignorant of what greatness is promised thee. Lay it
  15. to thy heart, and farewell.'
  16. Glamis thou art, and Cawdor; and shalt be
  17. What thou art promised: yet do I fear thy nature;
  18. It is too full o' the milk of human kindness
  19. To catch the nearest way: thou wouldst be great;
  20. Art not without ambition, but without
  21. The illness should attend it: what thou wouldst highly,
  22. That wouldst thou holily; wouldst not play false,
  23. And yet wouldst wrongly win: thou'ldst have, great Glamis,
  24. That which cries 'Thus thou must do, if thou have it;
  25. And that which rather thou dost fear to do
  26. Than wishest should be undone.' Hie thee hither,
  27. That I may pour my spirits in thine ear;
  28. And chastise with the valour of my tongue
  29. All that impedes thee from the golden round,
  30. Which fate and metaphysical aid doth seem
  31. To have thee crown'd withal.
  32. Enter a Messenger
  33. What is your tidings?
  1. Messenger
  2. The king comes here to-night.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. Thou'rt mad to say it:
  3. Is not thy master with him? who, were't so,
  4. Would have inform'd for preparation.
  1. Messenger
  2. So please you, it is true: our thane is coming:
  3. One of my fellows had the speed of him,
  4. Who, almost dead for breath, had scarcely more
  5. Than would make up his message.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. Give him tending;
  3. He brings great news.
  4. Exit Messenger
  5. The raven himself is hoarse
  6. That croaks the fatal entrance of Duncan
  7. Under my battlements. Come, you spirits
  8. That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here,
  9. And fill me from the crown to the toe top-full
  10. Of direst cruelty! make thick my blood;
  11. Stop up the access and passage to remorse,
  12. That no compunctious visitings of nature
  13. Shake my fell purpose, nor keep peace between
  14. The effect and it! Come to my woman's breasts,
  15. And take my milk for gall, you murdering ministers,
  16. Wherever in your sightless substances
  17. You wait on nature's mischief! Come, thick night,
  18. And pall thee in the dunnest smoke of hell,
  19. That my keen knife see not the wound it makes,
  20. Nor heaven peep through the blanket of the dark,
  21. To cry 'Hold, hold!'
  22. Enter MACBETH
  23. Great Glamis! worthy Cawdor!
  24. Greater than both, by the all-hail hereafter!
  25. Thy letters have transported me beyond
  26. This ignorant present, and I feel now
  27. The future in the instant.
  1. MACBETH
  2. My dearest love,
  3. Duncan comes here to-night.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. And when goes hence?
  1. MACBETH
  2. To-morrow, as he purposes.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. O, never
  3. Shall sun that morrow see!
  4. Your face, my thane, is as a book where men
  5. May read strange matters. To beguile the time,
  6. Look like the time; bear welcome in your eye,
  7. Your hand, your tongue: look like the innocent flower,
  8. But be the serpent under't. He that's coming
  9. Must be provided for: and you shall put
  10. This night's great business into my dispatch;
  11. Which shall to all our nights and days to come
  12. Give solely sovereign sway and masterdom.
  1. MACBETH
  2. We will speak further.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. Only look up clear;
  3. To alter favour ever is to fear:
  4. Leave all the rest to me.
Exeunt

SCENE VI. Before Macbeth's castle.

Hautboys and torches. Enter DUNCAN, MALCOLM, DONALBAIN, BANQUO, LENNOX, MACDUFF, ROSS, ANGUS, and Attendants
  1. DUNCAN
  2. This castle hath a pleasant seat; the air
  3. Nimbly and sweetly recommends itself
  4. Unto our gentle senses.
  1. BANQUO
  2. This guest of summer,
  3. The temple-haunting martlet, does approve,
  4. By his loved mansionry, that the heaven's breath
  5. Smells wooingly here: no jutty, frieze,
  6. Buttress, nor coign of vantage, but this bird
  7. Hath made his pendent bed and procreant cradle:
  8. Where they most breed and haunt, I have observed,
  9. The air is delicate.
Enter LADY MACBETH
  1. DUNCAN
  2. See, see, our honour'd hostess!
  3. The love that follows us sometime is our trouble,
  4. Which still we thank as love. Herein I teach you
  5. How you shall bid God 'ild us for your pains,
  6. And thank us for your trouble.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. All our service
  3. In every point twice done and then done double
  4. Were poor and single business to contend
  5. Against those honours deep and broad wherewith
  6. Your majesty loads our house: for those of old,
  7. And the late dignities heap'd up to them,
  8. We rest your hermits.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. Where's the thane of Cawdor?
  3. We coursed him at the heels, and had a purpose
  4. To be his purveyor: but he rides well;
  5. And his great love, sharp as his spur, hath holp him
  6. To his home before us. Fair and noble hostess,
  7. We are your guest to-night.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. Your servants ever
  3. Have theirs, themselves and what is theirs, in compt,
  4. To make their audit at your highness' pleasure,
  5. Still to return your own.
  1. DUNCAN
  2. Give me your hand;
  3. Conduct me to mine host: we love him highly,
  4. And shall continue our graces towards him.
  5. By your leave, hostess.
Exeunt

SCENE VII. Macbeth's castle.

Hautboys and torches. Enter a Sewer, and divers Servants with dishes and service, and pass over the stage. Then enter MACBETH
  1. MACBETH
  2. If it were done when 'tis done, then 'twere well
  3. It were done quickly: if the assassination
  4. Could trammel up the consequence, and catch
  5. With his surcease success; that but this blow
  6. Might be the be-all and the end-all here,
  7. But here, upon this bank and shoal of time,
  8. We'ld jump the life to come. But in these cases
  9. We still have judgment here; that we but teach
  10. Bloody instructions, which, being taught, return
  11. To plague the inventor: this even-handed justice
  12. Commends the ingredients of our poison'd chalice
  13. To our own lips. He's here in double trust;

  19. So clear in his great office, that his virtues
  20. Will plead like angels, trumpet-tongued, against

  22. And pity, like a naked new-born babe,
  23. Striding the blast, or heaven's cherubim, horsed
  24. Upon the sightless couriers of the air,
  25. Shall blow the horrid deed in every eye,

  27. To prick the sides of my intent, but only
  28. Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself

  1. MACBETH

  4. Golden opinions from all sorts of people,
  5. Which would be worn now in their newest gloss,
  6. Not cast aside so soon.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. Was the hope drunk
  3. Wherein you dress'd yourself? hath it slept since?

  5. At what it did so freely? From this time
  6. Such I account thy love. Art thou afeard
  7. To be the same in thine own act and valour

  9. Which thou esteem'st the ornament of life,
  10. And live a coward in thine own esteem,
  11. Letting 'I dare not' wait upon 'I would,'
  12. Like the poor cat i' the adage?
  1. MACBETH
  2. Prithee, peace:
  3. I dare do all that may become a man;
  4. Who dares do more is none.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. What beast was't, then,
  3. That made you break this enterprise to me?

  5. And, to be more than what you were, you would
  6. Be so much more the man. Nor time nor place
  7. Did then adhere, and yet you would make both:
  8. They have made themselves, and that their fitness now
  9. Does unmake you. I have given suck, and know
  10. How tender 'tis to love the babe that milks me:
  11. I would, while it was smiling in my face,
  12. Have pluck'd my nipple from his boneless gums,
  13. And dash'd the brains out, had I so sworn as you
  14. Have done to this.
  1. MACBETH
  2. If we should fail?
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. We fail!

  4. And we'll not fail. When Duncan is asleep —
  5. Whereto the rather shall his day's hard journey
  6. Soundly invite him — his two chamberlains
  7. Will I with wine and wassail so convince
  8. That memory, the warder of the brain,
  9. Shall be a fume, and the receipt of reason
  10. A limbeck only: when in swinish sleep
  11. Their drenched natures lie as in a death,
  12. What cannot you and I perform upon
  13. The unguarded Duncan? what not put upon
  14. His spongy officers, who shall bear the guilt
  15. Of our great quell?
  1. MACBETH
  2. Bring forth men-children only;
  3. For thy undaunted mettle should compose
  4. Nothing but males. Will it not be received,
  5. When we have mark'd with blood those sleepy two
  6. Of his own chamber and used their very daggers,
  7. That they have done't?
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. Who dares receive it other,
  3. As we shall make our griefs and clamour roar
  4. Upon his death?
  1. MACBETH
  2. I am settled, and bend up
  3. Each corporal agent to this terrible feat.
  4. Away, and mock the time with fairest show:
  5. False face must hide what the false heart doth know.

  9. Deal between thee and me! for even now
  10. I put myself to thy direction, and
  11. Unspeak mine own detraction, here abjure
  12. The taints and blames I laid upon myself,
  13. For strangers to my nature. I am yet
  14. Unknown to woman, never was forsworn,
  15. Scarcely have coveted what was mine own,
  16. At no time broke my faith, would not betray
  17. The devil to his fellow and delight
  18. No less in truth than life: my first false speaking
  19. Was this upon myself: what I am truly,
  20. Is thine and my poor country's to command:
  21. Whither indeed, before thy here-approach,
  22. Old Siward, with ten thousand warlike men,
  23. Already at a point, was setting forth.
  24. Now we'll together; and the chance of goodness
  25. Be like our warranted quarrel! Why are you silent?
  1. MACDUFF
  2. Such welcome and unwelcome things at once
  3. 'Tis hard to reconcile.
Enter a Doctor
  1. MALCOLM
  2. Well; more anon. — Comes the king forth, I pray you?
  1. Doctor
  2. Ay, sir; there are a crew of wretched souls
  3. That stay his cure: their malady convinces
  4. The great assay of art; but at his touch —
  5. Such sanctity hath heaven given his hand —
  6. They presently amend.
  1. MALCOLM
  2. I thank you, doctor.
Exit Doctor
  1. MACDUFF
  2. What's the disease he means?
  1. MALCOLM
  2. 'Tis call'd the evil:
  3. A most miraculous work in this good king;
  4. Which often, since my here-remain in England,
  5. I have seen him do. How he solicits heaven,
  6. Himself best knows: but strangely-visited people,
  7. All swoln and ulcerous, pitiful to the eye,
  8. The mere despair of surgery, he cures,
  9. Hanging a golden stamp about their necks,
  10. Put on with holy prayers: and 'tis spoken,
  11. To the succeeding royalty he leaves
  12. The healing benediction. With this strange virtue,
  13. He hath a heavenly gift of prophecy,
  14. And sundry blessings hang about his throne,
  15. That speak him full of grace.
Enter ROSS
  1. MACDUFF
  2. See, who comes here?
  1. MALCOLM
  2. My countryman; but yet I know him not.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. My ever-gentle cousin, welcome hither.
  1. MALCOLM
  2. I know him now. Good God, betimes remove
  3. The means that makes us strangers!
  1. ROSS
  2. Sir, amen.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. Stands Scotland where it did?
  1. ROSS
  2. Alas, poor country!
  3. Almost afraid to know itself. It cannot
  4. Be call'd our mother, but our grave; where nothing,
  5. But who knows nothing, is once seen to smile;
  6. Where sighs and groans and shrieks that rend the air
  7. Are made, not mark'd; where violent sorrow seems
  8. A modern ecstasy; the dead man's knell
  9. Is there scarce ask'd for who; and good men's lives
  10. Expire before the flowers in their caps,
  11. Dying or ere they sicken.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. O, relation
  3. Too nice, and yet too true!
  1. MALCOLM
  2. What's the newest grief?
  1. ROSS
  2. That of an hour's age doth hiss the speaker:
  3. Each minute teems a new one.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. How does my wife?
  1. ROSS
  2. Why, well.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. And all my children?
  1. ROSS
  2. Well too.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. The tyrant has not batter'd at their peace?
  1. ROSS
  2. No; they were well at peace when I did leave 'em.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. But not a niggard of your speech: how goes't?
  1. ROSS
  2. When I came hither to transport the tidings,
  3. Which I have heavily borne, there ran a rumour
  4. Of many worthy fellows that were out;
  5. Which was to my belief witness'd the rather,
  6. For that I saw the tyrant's power a-foot:
  7. Now is the time of help; your eye in Scotland
  8. Would create soldiers, make our women fight,
  9. To doff their dire distresses.
  1. MALCOLM
  2. Be't their comfort
  3. We are coming thither: gracious England hath
  4. Lent us good Siward and ten thousand men;
  5. An older and a better soldier none
  6. That Christendom gives out.
  1. ROSS
  2. Would I could answer
  3. This comfort with the like! But I have words
  4. That would be howl'd out in the desert air,
  5. Where hearing should not latch them.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. What concern they?
  3. The general cause? or is it a fee-grief
  4. Due to some single breast?
  1. ROSS
  2. No mind that's honest
  3. But in it shares some woe; though the main part
  4. Pertains to you alone.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. If it be mine,
  3. Keep it not from me, quickly let me have it.
  1. ROSS
  2. Let not your ears despise my tongue for ever,
  3. Which shall possess them with the heaviest sound
  4. That ever yet they heard.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. Hum! I guess at it.
  1. ROSS
  2. Your castle is surprised; your wife and babes
  3. Savagely slaughter'd: to relate the manner,
  4. Were, on the quarry of these murder'd deer,
  5. To add the death of you.
  1. MALCOLM
  2. Merciful heaven!
  3. What, man! ne'er pull your hat upon your brows;
  4. Give sorrow words: the grief that does not speak
  5. Whispers the o'er-fraught heart and bids it break.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. My children too?
  1. ROSS
  2. Wife, children, servants, all
  3. That could be found.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. And I must be from thence!
  3. My wife kill'd too?
  1. ROSS
  2. I have said.
  1. MALCOLM
  2. Be comforted:
  3. Let's make us medicines of our great revenge,
  4. To cure this deadly grief.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. He has no children. All my pretty ones?
  3. Did you say all? O hell-kite! All?
  4. What, all my pretty chickens and their dam
  5. At one fell swoop?
  1. MALCOLM
  2. Dispute it like a man.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. I shall do so;
  3. But I must also feel it as a man:
  4. I cannot but remember such things were,
  5. That were most precious to me. Did heaven look on,
  6. And would not take their part? Sinful Macduff,
  7. They were all struck for thee! naught that I am,
  8. Not for their own demerits, but for mine,
  9. Fell slaughter on their souls. Heaven rest them now!
  1. MALCOLM
  2. Be this the whetstone of your sword: let grief
  3. Convert to anger; blunt not the heart, enrage it.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. O, I could play the woman with mine eyes
  3. And braggart with my tongue! But, gentle heavens,
  4. Cut short all intermission; front to front
  5. Bring thou this fiend of Scotland and myself;
  6. Within my sword's length set him; if he 'scape,
  7. Heaven forgive him too!
  1. MALCOLM
  2. This tune goes manly.
  3. Come, go we to the king; our power is ready;
  4. Our lack is nothing but our leave; Macbeth
  5. Is ripe for shaking, and the powers above
  6. Put on their instruments. Receive what cheer you may:
  7. The night is long that never finds the day.
Exeunt

ACT V

SCENE I. Dunsinane. Ante-room in the castle.

Enter a Doctor of Physic and a Waiting-Gentlewoman
  1. Doctor
  2. I have two nights watched with you, but can perceive
  3. no truth in your report. When was it she last walked?
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. Since his majesty went into the field, I have seen
  3. her rise from her bed, throw her night-gown upon
  4. her, unlock her closet, take forth paper, fold it,
  5. write upon't, read it, afterwards seal it, and again
  6. return to bed; yet all this while in a most fast sleep.
  1. Doctor
  2. A great perturbation in nature, to receive at once
  3. the benefit of sleep, and do the effects of
  4. watching! In this slumbery agitation, besides her
  5. walking and other actual performances, what, at any
  6. time, have you heard her say?
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. That, sir, which I will not report after her.
  1. Doctor
  2. You may to me: and 'tis most meet you should.
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. Neither to you nor any one; having no witness to
  3. confirm my speech.
  4. Enter LADY MACBETH, with a taper
  5. Lo you, here she comes! This is her very guise;
  6. and, upon my life, fast asleep. Observe her; stand close.
  1. Doctor
  2. How came she by that light?
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. Why, it stood by her: she has light by her
  3. continually; 'tis her command.
  1. Doctor
  2. You see, her eyes are open.
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. Ay, but their sense is shut.
  1. Doctor
  2. What is it she does now? Look, how she rubs her hands.
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. It is an accustomed action with her, to seem thus
  3. washing her hands: I have known her continue in
  4. this a quarter of an hour.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. Yet here's a spot.
  1. Doctor
  2. Hark! she speaks: I will set down what comes from
  3. her, to satisfy my remembrance the more strongly.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. Out, damned spot! out, I say! — One: two: why,
  3. then, 'tis time to do't. — Hell is murky! — Fie, my
  4. lord, fie! a soldier, and afeard? What need we
  5. fear who knows it, when none can call our power to
  6. account? — Yet who would have thought the old man
  7. to have had so much blood in him.
  1. Doctor
  2. Do you mark that?
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. The thane of Fife had a wife: where is she now? —
  3. What, will these hands ne'er be clean? — No more o'
  4. that, my lord, no more o' that: you mar all with
  5. this starting.
  1. Doctor
  2. Go to, go to; you have known what you should not.
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. She has spoke what she should not, I am sure of
  3. that: heaven knows what she has known.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. Here's the smell of the blood still: all the
  3. perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little
  4. hand. Oh, oh, oh!
  1. Doctor
  2. What a sigh is there! The heart is sorely charged.
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. I would not have such a heart in my bosom for the
  3. dignity of the whole body.
  1. Doctor
  2. Well, well, well —
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. Pray God it be, sir.
  1. Doctor
  2. This disease is beyond my practise: yet I have known
  3. those which have walked in their sleep who have died
  4. holily in their beds.
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. Wash your hands, put on your nightgown; look not so
  3. pale. — I tell you yet again, Banquo's buried; he
  4. cannot come out on's grave.
  1. Doctor
  2. Even so?
  1. LADY MACBETH
  2. To bed, to bed! there's knocking at the gate:
  3. come, come, come, come, give me your hand. What's
  4. done cannot be undone. — To bed, to bed, to bed!
Exit
  1. Doctor
  2. Will she go now to bed?
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. Directly.
  1. Doctor
  2. Foul whisperings are abroad: unnatural deeds
  3. Do breed unnatural troubles: infected minds
  4. To their deaf pillows will discharge their secrets:
  5. More needs she the divine than the physician.
  6. God, God forgive us all! Look after her;
  7. Remove from her the means of all annoyance,
  8. And still keep eyes upon her. So, good night:
  9. My mind she has mated, and amazed my sight.
  10. I think, but dare not speak.
  1. Gentlewoman
  2. Good night, good doctor.
Exeunt

  4. Revenges burn in them; for their dear causes
  5. Would to the bleeding and the grim alarm
  6. Excite the mortified man.
  1. ANGUS
  2. Near Birnam wood
  3. Shall we well meet them; that way are they coming.
  1. CAITHNESS
  2. Who knows if Donalbain be with his brother?
  1. LENNOX
  2. For certain, sir, he is not: I have a file
  3. Of all the gentry: there is Siward's son,
  4. And many unrough youths that even now
  5. Protest their first of manhood.
  1. MENTEITH
  2. What does the tyrant?
  1. CAITHNESS
  2. Great Dunsinane he strongly fortifies:
  3. Some say he's mad; others that lesser hate him
  4. Do call it valiant fury: but, for certain,
  5. He cannot buckle his distemper'd cause
  6. Within the belt of rule.
  1. ANGUS
  2. Now does he feel
  3. His secret murders sticking on his hands;
  4. Now minutely revolts upbraid his faith-breach;
  5. Those he commands move only in command,
  6. Nothing in love: now does he feel his title
  7. Hang loose about him, like a giant's robe
  8. Upon a dwarfish thief.
  1. MENTEITH
  2. Who then shall blame
  3. His pester'd senses to recoil and start,
  4. When all that is within him does condemn
  5. Itself for being there?
  1. CAITHNESS
  2. Well, march we on,
  3. To give obedience where 'tis truly owed:
  4. Meet we the medicine of the sickly weal,

  1. LENNOX
  2. Or so much as it needs,

  4. I cannot taint with fear. What's the boy Malcolm?

  6. All mortal consequences have pronounced me thus:
  7. 'Fear not, Macbeth; no man that's born of woman

  10. And mingle with the English epicures:
  11. The mind I sway by and the heart I bear
  12. Shall never sag with doubt nor shake with fear.

  14. The devil damn thee black, thou cream-faced loon!
  15. Where got'st thou that goose look?
  1. Servant
  2. There is ten thousand —
  1. MACBETH
  2. Geese, villain!
  1. Servant
  2. Soldiers, sir.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Go prick thy face, and over-red thy fear,

  1. Servant
  2. The English force, so please you.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Take thy face hence.
  3. Exit Servant

  8. Is fall'n into the sear, the yellow leaf;
  9. And that which should accompany old age,
  10. As honour, love, obedience, troops of friends,
  11. I must not look to have; but, in their stead,
  12. Curses, not loud but deep, mouth-honour, breath,
  13. Which the poor heart would fain deny, and dare not. Seyton!

  1. SEYTON
  2. What is your gracious pleasure?
  1. MACBETH
  2. What news more?
  1. SEYTON
  2. All is confirm'd, my lord, which was reported.
  1. MACBETH
  2. I'll fight till from my bones my flesh be hack'd.
  3. Give me my armour.
  1. SEYTON
  2. 'Tis not needed yet.
  1. MACBETH
  2. I'll put it on.

  4. Hang those that talk of fear. Give me mine armour.
  5. How does your patient, doctor?
  1. Doctor
  2. Not so sick, my lord,
  3. As she is troubled with thick coming fancies,
  4. That keep her from her rest.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Cure her of that.
  3. Canst thou not minister to a mind diseased,
  4. Pluck from the memory a rooted sorrow,
  5. Raze out the written troubles of the brain
  6. And with some sweet oblivious antidote
  7. Cleanse the stuff'd bosom of that perilous stuff
  8. Which weighs upon the heart?
  1. Doctor
  2. Therein the patient
  3. Must minister to himself.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Throw physic to the dogs; I'll none of it.

  6. The water of my land, find her disease,

  8. I would applaud thee to the very echo,
  9. That should applaud again. — Pull't off, I say. —
  10. What rhubarb, cyme, or what purgative drug,
  11. Would scour these English hence? Hear'st thou of them?
  1. Doctor
  2. Ay, my good lord; your royal preparation
  3. Makes us hear something.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Bring it after me.
  3. I will not be afraid of death and bane,
  4. Till Birnam forest come to Dunsinane.
  1. Doctor
  2. Aside Were I from Dunsinane away and clear,
  3. Profit again should hardly draw me here.
Exeunt

  5. Would at a dismal treatise rouse and stir

  10. That struts and frets his hour upon the stage

  2. Gracious my lord,

  1. Messenger

  2. Liar and slave!
  1. Messenger

  6. And little is to do.
  1. MALCOLM
  2. We have met with foes
  3. That strike beside us.
  1. SIWARD
  2. Enter, sir, the castle.
Exeunt. Alarums

SCENE VIII. Another part of the field.

Enter MACBETH
  1. MACBETH
  2. Why should I play the Roman fool, and die
  3. On mine own sword? whiles I see lives, the gashes
  4. Do better upon them.
Enter MACDUFF
  1. MACDUFF
  2. Turn, hell-hound, turn!
  1. MACBETH
  2. Of all men else I have avoided thee:
  3. But get thee back; my soul is too much charged
  4. With blood of thine already.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. I have no words:
  3. My voice is in my sword: thou bloodier villain
  4. Than terms can give thee out!
They fight
  1. MACBETH
  2. Thou losest labour:
  3. As easy mayst thou the intrenchant air
  4. With thy keen sword impress as make me bleed:
  5. Let fall thy blade on vulnerable crests;
  6. I bear a charmed life, which must not yield,
  7. To one of woman born.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. Despair thy charm;
  3. And let the angel whom thou still hast served
  4. Tell thee, Macduff was from his mother's womb
  5. Untimely ripp'd.
  1. MACBETH
  2. Accursed be that tongue that tells me so,
  3. For it hath cow'd my better part of man!
  4. And be these juggling fiends no more believed,
  5. That palter with us in a double sense;
  6. That keep the word of promise to our ear,
  7. And break it to our hope. I'll not fight with thee.
  1. MACDUFF
  2. Then yield thee, coward,
  3. And live to be the show and gaze o' the time:
  4. We'll have thee, as our rarer monsters are,
  5. Painted on a pole, and underwrit,
  6. 'Here may you see the tyrant.'
  1. MACBETH
  2. I will not yield,
  3. To kiss the ground before young Malcolm's feet,
  4. And to be baited with the rabble's curse.
  5. Though Birnam wood be come to Dunsinane,
  6. And thou opposed, being of no woman born,
  7. Yet I will try the last. Before my body
  8. I throw my warlike shield. Lay on, Macduff,
  9. And damn'd be him that first cries, 'Hold, enough!'
Exeunt, fighting. Alarums
Retreat. Flourish. Enter, with drum and colours, MALCOLM, SIWARD, ROSS, the other Thanes, and Soldiers
  1. MALCOLM
  2. I would the friends we miss were safe arrived.
  1. SIWARD
  2. Some must go off: and yet, by these I see,
  3. So great a day as this is cheaply bought.
  1. MALCOLM
  2. Macduff is missing, and your noble son.
  1. ROSS
  2. Your son, my lord, has paid a soldier's debt:
  3. He only lived but till he was a man;
  4. The which no sooner had his prowess confirm'd
  5. In the unshrinking station where he fought,
  6. But like a man he died.
  1. SIWARD
  2. Then he is dead?
  1. ROSS
  2. Ay, and brought off the field: your cause of sorrow
  3. Must not be measured by his worth, for then
  4. It hath no end.
  1. SIWARD
  2. Had he his hurts before?
  1. ROSS
  2. Ay, on the front.
  1. SIWARD
  2. Why then, God's soldier be he!
  3. Had I as many sons as I have hairs,
  4. I would not wish them to a fairer death:
  5. And so, his knell is knoll'd.
  1. MALCOLM
  2. He's worth more sorrow,
  3. And that I'll spend for him.
  1. SIWARD
  2. He's worth no more
  3. They say he parted well, and paid his score:
  4. And so, God be with him! Here comes newer comfort.
Re-enter MACDUFF, with MACBETH's head
  1. MACDUFF
  2. Hail, king! for so thou art: behold, where stands
  3. The usurper's cursed head: the time is free:
  4. I see thee compass'd with thy kingdom's pearl,
  5. That speak my salutation in their minds;
  6. Whose voices I desire aloud with mine:
  7. Hail, King of Scotland!
  1. ALL
  2. Hail, King of Scotland!
Flourish
  1. MALCOLM
  2. We shall not spend a large expense of time
  3. Before we reckon with your several loves,
  4. And make us even with you. My thanes and kinsmen,
  5. Henceforth be earls, the first that ever Scotland
  6. In such an honour named. What's more to do,
  7. Which would be planted newly with the time,
  8. As calling home our exiled friends abroad
  9. That fled the snares of watchful tyranny;
  10. Producing forth the cruel ministers
  11. Of this dead butcher and his fiend-like queen,
  12. Who, as 'tis thought, by self and violent hands
  13. Took off her life; this, and what needful else
  14. That calls upon us, by the grace of Grace,
  15. We will perform in measure, time and place:
  16. So, thanks to all at once and to each one,
  17. Whom we invite to see us crown'd at Scone.
Flourish. Exeunt
